# NeRF

*source: arxiv_pdf | tokens: 12821*

Mildenhall et al. (2020) introduce Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF), a method for novel view synthesis that represents scenes as continuous 5D functions optimized via gradient descent. Addressing the limitations of discrete voxel grids and mesh-based approaches which struggle with resolution and storage, NeRF maps a sparse set of input views to a continuous volumetric scene representation using a fully-connected multilayer perceptron (MLP). The network accepts a 5D coordinate (spatial location x, y, z and viewing direction θ, φ) and outputs volume density σ and view-dependent RGB color c. The architecture consists of 8 fully-connected layers with 256 channels and ReLU activations. To overcome the spectral bias of MLPs, the authors employ positional encoding, mapping inputs to a higher-dimensional space via sinusoidal functions (L=10 for spatial, L=4 for directional), and a hierarchical sampling strategy using coarse and fine networks to allocate queries efficiently. Rendering is achieved through differentiable volume rendering, allowing end-to-end optimization from RGB images without 3D supervision. For real scenes, they utilize Normalized Device Coordinates (NDC) to handle infinite depth.

Evaluated on the DeepVoxels (Diffuse and Realistic Synthetic 360) and LLFF (Real Forward-Facing) datasets, NeRF achieves state-of-the-art results. On the Realistic Synthetic 360 dataset, NeRF attains a PSNR of 31.01, outperforming Neural Volumes (NV) at 26.05 and Local Light Field Fusion (LLFF) at 24.88. On Diffuse Synthetic 360, it reaches 40.15 PSNR compared to LLFF's 34.38. For real-world forward-facing scenes, NeRF scores 26.50 PSNR against LLFF's 24.13. The method requires only RGB images and camera poses, optimizing a separate network per scene. Ablation studies confirm that positional encoding and view-dependent radiance are critical for high-frequency detail, while hierarchical sampling improves efficiency.

Despite its quality, NeRF has significant computational limitations. Training a single scene requires 100–300k iterations on an NVIDIA V100, taking 1–2 days, and inference takes approximately 30 seconds per frame. The storage cost is low (5 MB weights) compared to LLFF's voxel grids (15 GB), but the training latency is high. In the context of set-based neural architectures, NeRF treats the input views as a set to learn a continuous function, distinguishing it from permutation-invariant set networks but sharing the capability to aggregate information from unordered observations to reconstruct a global representation. Future work is suggested to improve optimization efficiency and interpretability of the learned weights.
