# A Survey of Convolutional Neural Networks: Analysis, Applications, and Prospects

*source: s2_fulltext | tokens: 25146*

Li et al. (2021) present a comprehensive survey of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) in *IEEE Transactions on Neural Networks and Learning Systems*. Unlike prior reviews focusing narrowly on applications (e.g., Rawat et al. on image recognition, Ajmal et al. on segmentation), this work adopts a general perspective, analyzing foundational components alongside architecture evolution. The taxonomy spans historical development from LeNet-5 to modern lightweight models like GhostNet and MobileNet v3, covering 1D, 2D, and 3D convolutional applications across time-series, vision, and signal processing.

The authors provide empirical analysis on activation functions, loss functions, and optimizers using MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet. Key findings include ReLU and Leaky ReLU outperforming Sigmoid/Tanh in hidden layers due to gradient vanishing issues, with ELU showing slightly higher accuracy but slower convergence. In architecture design, the survey highlights VGG’s use of stacked 3x3 kernels reducing parameters by 45% compared to 5x5 kernels, and ShuffleNet v2’s guidelines prioritizing Memory Access Cost (MAC) over FLOPs for mobile efficiency, noting that equal input/output channels minimize MAC.

This survey distinguishes itself from earlier works (e.g., Aloysius et al., Dhillon et al.) by including post-2019 innovations and offering "rules of thumb" derived from experiments rather than theoretical overviews. For a survey on Set Neural Networks, this paper serves as a critical baseline, explicitly identifying CNN limitations such as spatial information loss and lack of equivariance to rotation/scaling. These identified gaps motivate the development of alternative inductive biases, such as Capsule Networks (discussed in the "Prospects" section) or set-based processing methods that address permutation invariance. While not focused on Set NNs, the paper’s systematic critique of CNN invariance properties provides essential context for understanding why set-based architectures are necessary for certain tasks.

The "Prospects" section further outlines trends in model compression (pruning, quantization), security (adversarial attacks, backdoors), and Network Architecture Search (NAS). The discussion on Capsule Networks specifically notes their potential to overcome CNN's pooling-induced spatial information loss, a key argument often paralleled in Set Neural Network literature regarding the need for structured feature aggregation. Limitations include a broad scope that precludes deep dives into specific niche architectures. Nevertheless, it remains a definitive reference for CNN evolution and component selection, essential for contextualizing the shift toward set-based or capsule-based alternatives in modern deep learning.
