# Graph neural networks: A review of methods and applications

*source: arxiv_pdf | tokens: 33768*

Zhou et al. (2021) present a comprehensive survey on Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), establishing them as the dominant paradigm for set-based deep learning on non-Euclidean data. The authors propose a general design pipeline for constructing GNNs, consisting of four steps: identifying graph structure, specifying graph type and scale, designing loss functions, and building models using computational modules. This framework highlights the flexibility of set processing, where nodes are treated as a set of elements aggregated via message passing.

The survey categorizes GNN variants by computational modules. Propagation modules include spectral approaches (ChebNet, GCN) and spatial approaches (GraphSAGE, GAT), alongside recurrent operators (GGNN, Graph LSTM) and skip connections (JK-Net, DeepGCNs) to mitigate over-smoothing. Sampling modules address scalability through node, layer, and subgraph sampling (ClusterGCN, GraphSAINT), while pooling modules (DiffPool, SAGPool) enable graph-level representation learning. The authors also detail frameworks unifying these operations, such as Message Passing Neural Networks (MPNN) and Graph Networks (GN).

Regarding datasets and benchmarks, the paper reviews standard citation networks (Cora, Citeseer, Pubmed) and chemical graphs (MUTAG, PPI) for node and graph classification tasks. It notably emphasizes the need for large-scale benchmarks, citing the Open Graph Benchmark (OGB) and scalable datasets from Dwivedi et al. (2020) to address the limitations of small-scale evaluations. Theoretical analyses cover expressivity limits relative to the Weisfeiler-Leman test and generalization bounds.

Applications are divided into structural scenarios (molecular fingerprints, knowledge graph completion, physics simulation) and non-structural scenarios (text classification, image reasoning), demonstrating GNNs' utility in processing sets derived from various domains. The survey concludes by identifying four open problems: robustness against adversarial attacks, interpretability, graph pretraining (self-supervised learning), and modeling complex structures (heterogeneous, dynamic graphs). For a survey on Set Neural Networks, this work provides essential context on the primary architecture processing unordered sets via permutation-invariant aggregation, while highlighting current limitations in scalability and theoretical expressivity.
