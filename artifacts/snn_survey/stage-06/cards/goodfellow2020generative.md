# Generative adversarial networks

*source: arxiv_pdf | tokens: 6074*

Goodfellow et al. (2014) introduced Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), proposing a novel framework for estimating generative models via an adversarial process. The authors address the persistent difficulty of training deep generative models, which typically rely on maximum likelihood estimation involving intractable probabilistic computations or require Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods for inference, leading to mixing problems in models like Restricted Boltzmann Machines (RBMs) and Deep Belief Networks (DBNs). Instead of optimizing a likelihood bound, the framework pits a generative model $G$ against a discriminative model $D$ in a minimax two-player game. $G$ (implemented as a multilayer perceptron) maps a prior noise distribution to data space, while $D$ estimates the probability that a sample came from the training data rather than $G$. The training objective minimizes $log(1 - D(G(z)))$ for the generator while maximizing $log D(x)$ for the discriminator.

Theoretically, the authors prove that in the non-parametric limit, the global optimum is reached when $G$ recovers the data distribution $p_{data}$ and $D$ outputs 1/2 everywhere, effectively minimizing the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the model and data distributions. Proposition 1 establishes the optimal discriminator $D^*_G(x) = p_{data}(x) / (p_{data}(x) + p_g(x))$, while Theorem 1 demonstrates that the global minimum of the training criterion is achieved if and only if $p_g = p_{data}$. The training procedure alternates between $k$ steps of optimizing $D$ and one step of optimizing $G$ to avoid overfitting and computational prohibitions.

Experiments were conducted on MNIST, the Toronto Face Database (TFD), and CIFAR-10. Using Parzen window-based log-likelihood estimates, the GAN achieved 225 ± 2 on MNIST and 2057 ± 26 on TFD, outperforming Deep Belief Networks (DBN: 138 on MNIST) and Stacked Contractive Autoencoders (Stacked CAE: 121 on MNIST). The framework eliminates the need for unrolled approximate inference networks or Markov chains during sampling, allowing the use of piecewise linear units (ReLU, maxout) which are difficult to use in feedback loops.

Key limitations include the lack of an explicit representation of the probability density $p_g(x)$ and training instability, specifically the risk of "mode collapse" (termed the "Helvetica scenario") where $G$ collapses to a single output. The paper also notes that evaluating likelihood remains difficult, requiring density estimation on generated samples. Unlike Noise-Contrastive Estimation (NCE), which uses a fixed noise distribution, GANs learn a discriminator dynamically. Despite these challenges, the paper establishes a new paradigm for generative modeling that relies on backpropagation rather than energy-based sampling. This capability to generate high-fidelity samples without Markov chains is particularly relevant for generative tasks involving structured data, providing a foundation for subsequent architectures processing sets of data points.
