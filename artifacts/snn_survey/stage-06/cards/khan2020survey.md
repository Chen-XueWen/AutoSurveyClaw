# A survey of the recent architectures of deep convolutional neural networks

*source: arxiv_pdf | tokens: 35634*

Khan et al. (2020) present a comprehensive survey of deep Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) architectures spanning from 2012 to 2020, establishing a systematic taxonomy for architectural innovations. The authors categorize recent advancements into seven intrinsic classes: spatial exploitation, depth, multi-path, width, feature-map exploitation, channel boosting, and attention-based processing. This classification traces the evolution from foundational models like LeNet-5 (MNIST error 0.95) and AlexNet (ImageNet error 16.4%) to state-of-the-art structures such as ResNet-152 (CIFAR-10 error 6.43%), DenseNet, and Xception. The survey emphasizes that significant performance gains stem from structural reforms—specifically residual connections in ResNet and attention mechanisms in Squeeze-and-Excitation Networks (SE-Net) and CBAM—rather than mere parameter optimization. Key datasets utilized for benchmarking include ImageNet, CIFAR-10/100, COCO, and medical datasets like MITOSIS. The authors note limitations regarding computational cost and memory requirements, driving research toward lightweight architectures like MobileNet and ShuffleNet to deploy on resource-constrained hardware. Furthermore, the paper addresses challenges such as the vanishing gradient problem, the "black box" nature of deep models, and the need for hardware acceleration via GPUs and TPUs. Applications span image classification, segmentation, object detection, NLP, and speech recognition. The work concludes that block-based design is the prevailing paradigm for enhancing representational capacity. While focused on CNNs, these architectural principles underpin many vision tasks that process image data as sets of features, making this survey relevant to broader discussions on structured data representation and set-based processing. Future directions include ensemble learning and generative capabilities to boost model robustness. The survey also details specific parameter counts, such as GoogLeNet's 4 million parameters versus VGG's 138 million, highlighting the trade-off between accuracy and efficiency. It further categorizes challenges associated with each architecture type, such as the high computational cost of Inception networks versus the parameter efficiency of ResNeXt.
