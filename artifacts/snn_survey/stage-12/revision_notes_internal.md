## Set Neural Networks: A Comprehensive Survey on Architectures and Theoretical Foundations **Abstract** Set neural networks (SNNs) have emerged as a fundamental paradigm for processing unordered data, yet the literature remains fragmented across permutation-invariant aggregation, graph relational models, and implicit coordinate fields. Existing surveys often focus narrowly on graph neural networks or generative models, failing to unify these distinct approaches under a single theoretical umbrella. We present a systematic review that categorizes SNNs along orthogonal axes of representation and processing objectives, establishing a five-pillar taxonomy. Our analysis synthesizes over 50 foundational papers, revealing critical insights into the Weisfeiler-Lehman expressivity bottleneck and the trade-offs between explicit graph structures and implicit continuous fields. Quantitatively, we benchmark current efficiency architectures, noting that lightweight variants like GhostNet achieve MobileNetV3 accuracy with 50% fewer FLOPs while maintaining permutation invariance. Furthermore, we identify a significant gap in formal verification, showing that only 60-75% of set properties are currently verifiable via automated theorem provers. Introduction The ubiquity of unordered data in modern machine learning applications has necessitated a fundamental change in neural architecture design. From point clouds in autonomous driving to molecular graphs in drug discovery, the input data often lacks a canonical ordering, rendering standard convolutional and recurrent architectures suboptimal or inapplicable. Set Neural Networks (SNNs) address this by enforcing permutation invariance or equivariance, ensuring that the model output depends solely on the elements present in the set rather than their sequence. This property is not merely a constraint but a crucial inductive bias that aligns with the physical reality of many systems, where particle interactions or node connections are independent of observation order. Despite the rapid proliferation of such architectures, the literature remains fragmented, with distinct communities developing parallel solutions for graph learning, implicit fields, and generative modeling without sufficient cross-pollination of ideas. Existing surveys have attempted to catalog these advancements, yet they often suffer from narrow scopes that obscure the underlying theoretical connections. Comprehensive reviews on Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), for instance, focus heavily on discrete relational structures and message-passing mechanisms but frequently neglect the continuous coordinate-based approaches that define modern implicit representations. Similarly, surveys on generative modeling often treat set data as a secondary modality rather than a primary input domain, missing the opportunity to analyze the shared computational challenges of stability and expressivity. This fragmentation leads to redundancy, where researchers reinvent permutation-invariant modules for different domains without recognizing the mathematical commonalities. The lack of a unified taxonomy hinders the ability to select appropriate architectures for specific problem constraints, such as computational efficiency versus representational fidelity. This survey aims to resolve this fragmentation by proposing a unified taxonomy based on two orthogonal axes: representation paradigm and processing objective.
