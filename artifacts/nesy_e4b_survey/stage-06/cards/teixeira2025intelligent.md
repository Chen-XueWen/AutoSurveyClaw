# Intelligent Supply Chain Management: A Systematic Literature Review on Artificial Intelligence Contributions

*source: abstract_only | tokens: 144*

This systematic literature review provides a comprehensive, updated synthesis of Artificial Intelligence (AI) applications within Supply Chain Management (SCM), specifically targeting the literature published between 2021 and 2024. The authors explicitly position this work to overcome limitations in prior surveys, which they critique for either excluding recent literature or failing to provide an integrated analysis across the diverse phases of the supply chain lifecycle.

Methodologically, the study adheres to the rigorous PRISMA framework, utilizing the Scopus database, complemented by bibliometric and content analyses to ensure systematic coverage. The review’s scope is highly structured, categorizing AI contributions across three primary functional themes: enhancing supply chain resilience, optimizing core processes, and promoting sustainability initiatives. Furthermore, it taxonomizes the underlying technologies, detailing the practical deployment of Machine Learning (ML), Deep Learning (DL), and the emerging capabilities of Generative AI (GenAI) within SCM contexts.

The key pattern identified is the shift from theoretical discussion to concrete implementation, showing how AI is moving beyond predictive modeling into generative and prescriptive roles. For instance, the review details applications ranging from using DL for demand forecasting to employing GenAI for simulating complex disruption scenarios, though specific quantitative benchmarks or model names are not detailed in the provided abstract. The primary contribution to the literature is the establishment of a contemporary, multi-faceted map of the field, thereby guiding future research by highlighting the integration challenges across these technological and functional domains. While the paper does not directly address neurosymbolic integration, its comprehensive mapping of functional gaps (e.g., the need for explainability in resilience modeling) provides a strong foundation for future research that must bridge the gap between data-driven pattern recognition (ML/DL) and formalized, rule-based knowledge representation (Symbolic AI).
