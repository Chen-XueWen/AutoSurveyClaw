# A review of explainable artificial intelligence in supply chain management using neurosymbolic approaches

*source: abstract_only | tokens: 152*

This paper functions as a **Survey / Review Paper**, providing a systematic and comprehensive overview of the intersection between Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) and Supply Chain Management (SCM), specifically focusing on methodologies utilizing Neurosymbolic AI. The central motivation driving the review is the critical barrier to widespread AI adoption in SCM: the lack of explainability in most existing AI models.

The authors employ a rigorous methodology, conducting a systematic review followed by bibliometric, descriptive, and thematic analyses of the identified literature. This approach allows them to map the evolution and current state of research in this niche area.

The core contribution is establishing a taxonomy and pattern recognition within the literature that centers on hybrid AI approaches—the Neurosymbolic spectrum. This framework posits that combining the pattern recognition power of neural architectures with the structured, verifiable reasoning capabilities of logic-based systems is essential for building trustworthy and explainable SCM decision support systems.

Key themes identified across the surveyed work include the necessity of integrating domain knowledge (symbolic reasoning) directly into deep learning models (neural networks) to move beyond "black-box" predictions. While the paper does not cite specific benchmark numbers, it highlights the *conceptual gap*: most current AI SCM applications fail to provide actionable explanations, limiting their practical utility for decision-makers.

This review is highly valuable for researchers entering the field, as it synthesizes disparate studies into a coherent structure. It positions Neurosymbolic AI as the current frontier for achieving both high predictive accuracy and necessary transparency within complex logistical and operational contexts. The paper implicitly frames future research directions by detailing the gaps in current implementations, suggesting a need for more standardized benchmarks and applied case studies demonstrating the efficacy of specific neurosymbolic components (e.g., integrating knowledge graphs with graph neural networks) within real-world SCM scenarios.
