# A Survey on Patent Analysis: From NLP to Multimodal AI

*source: arxiv_html | tokens: 11501*

This paper provides a comprehensive survey of the application of Artificial Intelligence, spanning Natural Language Processing (NLP) to Multimodal AI, within the specialized domain of patent analysis. The authors’ primary contribution is the introduction of a novel taxonomy that categorizes existing research methods based on the specific tasks within the patent life cycle, including Patent Classification, Patent Retrieval, Patent Quality Analysis, and Patent Generation. This structured overview aims to serve as a critical resource for researchers at the intersection of AI and intellectual property law.

The survey maps a clear methodological evolution. Early work relied on traditional neural networks (e.g., LSTM, Word2Vec) for tasks like classification (e.g., Grawe et al. (2017) using Word2Vec for IPC subgroup classification). The field has since advanced through ensemble models and the adoption of large-scale pre-trained models (PLMs). For instance, in classification, PLMs like BERT and RoBERTa have significantly improved performance over earlier methods, with Roudsari et al. (2022) achieving state-of-the-art results on the USPTO-2M dataset using fine-tuned BERT variants. In retrieval, multimodal techniques are highlighted, noting that combining vision transformers with text encoders (e.g., Pustu-Iren et al. (2021) using CLIP alongside RoBERTa) enhances semantic search capabilities.

The survey effectively identifies critical gaps, particularly concerning the integration of structured knowledge and legal reasoning. While LLMs (e.g., GPT-4V, LLaMA-3, as referenced in the literature) show immense potential for generation, the authors stress that current models suffer from hallucination and lack the nuanced understanding of human patent experts. This limitation points toward the need for methods that move beyond pure statistical correlation. The identified future directions strongly suggest a move toward symbolic integration: building Knowledge Graphs to structure patent relationships (e.g., citation networks, as seen with Liu and Li (2022)), and employing Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF). These advanced techniques are proposed to ground LLM outputs in verifiable, structured prior art, thereby enhancing both factual accuracy and legal robustness—a key area for neurosymbolic research.
