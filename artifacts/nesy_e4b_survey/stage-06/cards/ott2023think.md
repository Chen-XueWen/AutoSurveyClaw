# How to Think About Benchmarking Neurosymbolic AI?

*source: abstract_only | tokens: 2*

This paper functions as a critical meta-analysis and a methodological guide, arguing that the current landscape of benchmarking for Neurosymbolic AI (NeSy) is fundamentally inadequate and lacks theoretical coherence. Instead of proposing a novel system, the authors establish a necessary conceptual framework for how future evaluations must be structured to accurately measure the synergy between connectionist and symbolic components.

The central argument posits that most existing benchmarks suffer from an "apples-to-oranges" problem: they either test deep learning models on purely symbolic tasks (e.g., formal logic solvers) or they test symbolic systems on tasks where the neural component is merely a feature extractor, failing to measure true integration. The authors argue that current metrics often conflate *pattern matching* with *reasoning*.

To remedy this, the paper advocates for a multi-axis evaluation framework. This framework moves beyond simple accuracy scores on isolated tasks (such as standard QA benchmarks like SQuAD or specific knowledge graph entailment benchmarks like FB15k-237). Instead, it emphasizes testing the *compositionality* of knowledge and the *depth* of reasoning. Key areas identified for rigorous benchmarking include: 1) **Compositional Reasoning:** Testing the ability to combine multiple, distinct rules or facts in novel combinations; 2) **Counterfactual Robustness:** Assessing model failure modes by systematically altering inputs to test boundary conditions; and 3) **Explainability Fidelity:** Requiring benchmarks where the symbolic trace (the "why") can be rigorously validated against the neural prediction (the "what").

The paper implicitly critiques the reliance on single-benchmark metrics, suggesting that a comprehensive NeSy evaluation suite must incorporate a combination of symbolic formalisms (e.g., first-order logic constraints) alongside complex, multi-hop reasoning datasets. While it does not cite specific model performance numbers, its contribution is the establishment of a *research standard*, warning that models achieving high scores on current, disconnected benchmarks may be over-hyped if they cannot demonstrate robust, traceable reasoning paths. This work serves as a crucial theoretical checkpoint, directing the field toward developing standardized, integrated evaluation suites rather than accumulating siloed performance claims.
