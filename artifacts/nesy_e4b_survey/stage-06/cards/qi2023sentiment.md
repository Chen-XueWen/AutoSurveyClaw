# Sentiment analysis using Twitter data: a comparative application of lexicon- and machine-learning-based approach

*source: s2_fulltext | tokens: 11032*

Qi and Shabrina (2023) present a comparative empirical study on sentiment analysis using Twitter data during England's third Covid-19 lockdown (Jan–July 2021). The work addresses the challenge of selecting appropriate NLP techniques for social media text by benchmarking symbolic lexicon-based methods against statistical machine learning approaches. The dataset comprises 77,332 geotagged tweets from 10 major UK cities, filtered for Covid-19 keywords across three lockdown stages. Preprocessing included tokenization, stemming, and emoji conversion.

For the symbolic component, the authors evaluated TextBlob, VADER, and SentiWordNet. VADER emerged as the superior lexicon-based tool, effectively handling social media slang and abbreviations, whereas SentiWordNet exhibited bias toward negative sentiments due to limited domain coverage. For the statistical component, they trained Random Forest (RF), Multinomial Naive Bayes (MNB), and Support Vector Classification (SVC) classifiers using Bag of Words (BoW), TF-IDF, and Word2Vec feature representations. A subset of 3,000 tweets was manually annotated to serve as ground truth for supervised learning, with hyperparameters optimized via Random Search.

Results indicate that machine learning generally outperformed lexicon-based methods in classification tasks. The SVC classifier achieved the highest accuracy of 71% using BoW and TF-IDF features. However, performance varied by class; the RF classifier struggled with negative sentiment recall (0.25 with BoW), while SVC maintained balanced F1-scores across positive, negative, and neutral categories. Word2Vec embeddings performed poorly (accuracy ~53%), attributed to insufficient training data for effective vectorization. Sentiment analysis revealed that positive sentiment initially rose during lockdown easing but declined in the final stage, correlating with reduced vaccination rates and rising case numbers.

In the context of Neurosymbolic AI, this study serves as a critical baseline illustrating the distinct strengths and weaknesses of pure symbolic versus pure statistical paradigms. It highlights that while lexicon-based methods offer interpretability and rule-based reasoning, they lack flexibility with informal language. Conversely, ML methods capture context but require extensive labeled data and lack explicit reasoning capabilities. The authors conclude that hybrid approaches or improved lexicons could bridge this gap, implicitly supporting the neurosymbolic argument that integrating symbolic knowledge with statistical learning is necessary for robust NLP. Limitations include the reliance on Twitter data (location sharing bias) and the relatively small annotated dataset for training deep features.
