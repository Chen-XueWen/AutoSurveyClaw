# From statistical relational to neurosymbolic artificial intelligence: A survey

*source: arxiv_pdf | tokens: 31558*

Marra et al. (2024) present a comprehensive survey titled "From Statistical Relational to Neurosymbolic Artificial Intelligence: a Survey," aiming to unify the statistical relational AI (StarAI) and neurosymbolic AI (NeSy) communities. The authors argue that despite historical divergence—StarAI focusing on probabilistic graphical models and NeSy on neural networks—both fields share the fundamental goal of integrating learning and reasoning. The core contribution is a taxonomy of seven dimensions used to characterize and compare systems across both subfields: (1) logical inference approach (model vs. proof-based), (2) logical syntax (propositional, relational, FOL), (3) logical semantics (classical, fuzzy, probabilistic), (4) learning scope (parameter vs. structure), (5) representation type (symbolic vs. subsymbolic), (6) paradigm integration (logic, probability, neural), and (7) learning tasks.

The survey positions numerous frameworks along these dimensions to highlight analogies. For instance, proof-based NeSy systems like DeepProbLog and Neural Theorem Provers (NTP) are compared to directed StarAI models like ProbLog, while model-based NeSy approaches such as Logic Tensor Networks (LTN) and Semantic-Based Regularization (SBR) are aligned with undirected StarAI models like Markov Logic Networks (MLNs) and Probabilistic Soft Logic (PSL). The authors detail how these systems handle tasks ranging from knowledge graph completion (KGC) and distant supervision (e.g., MNIST addition) to generative modeling and knowledge induction. They specifically note that proof-based methods like KBANN and TensorLog use logic to define network architecture, whereas constraint-based methods like DL2 and SL use logic as a regularization loss.

Key themes identified include the trade-off between control/interpretability in proof-based architectures versus scalability in constraint-based loss functions, and the challenge of learning logical structure versus parameters. The survey concludes by outlining open challenges, including standardizing semantics (particularly fuzzy logic properties), improving scalable probabilistic inference, and addressing the combinatorial complexity of structure learning. Specific gaps highlighted include data efficiency (StarAI is data-efficient but doesn't scale; NeSy scales but is data-hungry) and symbolic representation learning. Unlike other surveys focusing on block composition or graph neural networks, this work emphasizes the underlying logical principles and computational semantics, offering a framework for cross-fertilization between the two communities. It explicitly contrasts itself with prior works like Besold et al. (2017) and Lamb et al. (2020), noting its unique focus on logical perspective and inference mechanisms rather than just high-level architectural patterns.
