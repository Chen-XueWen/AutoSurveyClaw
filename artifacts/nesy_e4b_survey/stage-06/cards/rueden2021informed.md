# Informed Machine Learning - A Taxonomy and Survey of Integrating Prior Knowledge into Learning Systems

*source: abstract_only | tokens: 156*

This paper, "Informed Machine Learning - A Taxonomy and Survey of Integrating Prior Knowledge into Learning Systems," serves as a foundational review for researchers tackling the limitations of purely data-driven models, particularly when training data is scarce or insufficient. Its primary contribution is not empirical results, but the establishment of a comprehensive conceptual framework and a rigorous taxonomy to structure the disparate body of work in the field, thereby providing necessary scaffolding for the burgeoning area of Neurosymbolic AI.

The authors define Informed Machine Learning (IML) as any learning paradigm that moves beyond pure pattern recognition by explicitly incorporating pre-existing knowledge. To achieve this, the paper proposes a detailed taxonomy that classifies IML approaches based on three orthogonal dimensions: the **source of knowledge** (e.g., symbolic rules, causal models, expert heuristics), the **representation** used (e.g., first-order logic, knowledge graphs, feature constraints), and the **integration point** within the ML pipeline (e.g., pre-training regularization, architectural modification, or post-hoc refinement).

By structuring the literature along these axes, the survey reveals key patterns: the historical trend moves from purely symbolic reasoning systems toward hybrid architectures that use knowledge to guide, constrain, or augment deep learning processes. The paper implicitly maps the tension between the statistical power of connectionist models and the interpretability/guarantees offered by symbolic knowledge.

The identified gap that this survey addresses is the lack of a unified classification system. Previous surveys often addressed specific knowledge sources (e.g., knowledge graph embedding) without providing a holistic view of *how* that knowledge interacts with the learning process. For the field of Neurosymbolic AI, this taxonomy is invaluable, as it allows researchers to precisely situate their proposed integration methods—for instance, distinguishing between knowledge used for *initialization* versus knowledge used for *loss function regularization*. The paper thus functions as a critical meta-review, guiding future research by providing a standardized vocabulary and a roadmap for systematically combining deep learning representations with formal, structured knowledge.
