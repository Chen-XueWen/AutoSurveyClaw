# DeBiasMe: De-biasing Human-AI Interactions with Metacognitive AIED (AI in Education) Interventions

*source: arxiv_html | tokens: 6962*

This position paper, "DeBiasMe," argues that current AI literacy frameworks are insufficient because they fail to account for the complex interplay between human cognitive biases and algorithmic biases in Generative AI (Gen AI) interactions. The central thesis posits that effective AI literacy must fundamentally incorporate **metacognitive skills**—the ability for users to monitor and regulate their own thinking—to transform students from passive consumers into active collaborators.

The authors identify a critical gap: existing tools focus predominantly on flagging *algorithmic* bias, neglecting the human element. They argue that biases such as anchoring and confirmation bias create a harmful feedback loop when interacting with LLMs (Glickman and Sharot, 2025). To address this, the paper advocates for a shift toward a **bi-directional Human-AI intervention** that intervenes at both the input (prompt formulation) and output (interpretation) stages.

The proposed conceptual framework relies on two key design principles: **deliberate friction** and **adaptive scaffolding**. Deliberate friction introduces intentional pauses, serving as metacognitive triggers to force reflection (e.g., Buçinca et al., 2021). The practical manifestation of this is the *DeBiasMe* prototype, an AIED intervention. At the input stage, it features a **Prompt Refinement Tool** that provides real-time feedback to help users detect and mitigate biases before querying the AI. At the output stage, a **Bias Visualization Map** visually diagrams identified biases (human, systematic, statistical, and computational) by connecting abstract bias types to specific text segments, making the abstract concrete.

For the field, the paper raises several open questions, particularly concerning the methodological integrity of bias detection. It suggests that future systems must adopt a **multi-model consensus approach** (Binns, 2018) and utilize progressive and adaptive visualization techniques (Bai et al., 2012) to manage cognitive load. While the paper focuses on LLMs, its implications extend to developing comprehensive bias measurement frameworks. Crucially, it implies that true AI literacy requires moving beyond technical competence to embed explicit, reflective, and self-aware reasoning processes, which aligns conceptually with the need for structured, symbolic scaffolding to augment the pattern-matching nature of large neural models.
