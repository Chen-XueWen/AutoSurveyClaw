# Integrating Neurosymbolic AI in Advanced Air Mobility: A Comprehensive Survey

*source: arxiv_html | tokens: 8482*

This paper, "Integrating Neurosymbolic AI in Advanced Air Mobility: A Comprehensive Survey," serves as a critical review mapping the intersection between Neurosymbolic Artificial Intelligence (NSAI) and the rapidly evolving field of Advanced Air Mobility (AAM). The authors position NSAI as the necessary "third wave of AI" (Garcez and Lamb, 2023) required to manage the inherent complexity, safety mandates, and regulatory hurdles of AAM systems, such as eVTOL operations.

The survey's scope is exceptionally broad, providing a detailed analysis across nearly every operational facet of AAM, including demand forecasting, aircraft design, real-time air traffic management (UTM), electrification, predictive maintenance, autonomy, cybersecurity, and data governance. The authors structure the review by detailing how NSAI's ability to fuse neural pattern recognition with symbolic, rule-based logic enhances decision-making in these disparate domains.

Conceptually, the paper reinforces existing NSAI taxonomies, noting approaches like *learning for reasoning*, *reasoning for learning*, and *learning-reasoning* (Yu et al., 2023). Key patterns emerge across the application areas: in **Safety** and **Autonomy**, NSAI is shown to be vital for embedding hard constraints—such as FAA regulations—to "shield" neural network outputs (Jansen et al., 2020; Sharifi et al., 2023). For **Cybersecurity**, NSAI enhances robustness by integrating formal security policies (Grov et al., 2024). Furthermore, in **Predictive Maintenance**, the combination of case-based reasoning (CBR) and probabilistic modeling (Yang et al., 2018) is highlighted.

The survey's primary contribution lies in synthesizing these isolated applications into a cohesive roadmap. It identifies significant gaps, which are not purely technical but deeply rooted in governance. These gaps include the lack of standardized certification protocols for adaptive AI (as noted by FAA/EASA roadmaps), the difficulty in assigning accountability in failure scenarios (Novelli et al., 2024), and the challenge of ensuring model fairness and mitigating bias. While prior literature has addressed AAM components in isolation (e.g., Long et al., 2023 for demand modeling), this work is positioned as the first to systematically connect NSAI across the *entire* AAM lifecycle, providing a comprehensive framework for future research.
