# Reasoning in Neurosymbolic AI

*source: arxiv_html | tokens: 29625*

This paper introduces the **Logical Boltzmann Machine (LBM)**, a novel energy-based neurosymbolic system designed to achieve a principled integration of logical reasoning and data-driven learning within neural networks. The central problem addressed is the inherent lack of formal reasoning guarantees and reliability observed in modern Large Language Models (LLMs). The technical core is establishing a formal equivalence: any propositional logic formula ($\varphi$) can be mapped to the energy function $E(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{h})$ of an RBM, such that the logical truth value $\text{s}_{\varphi}(\mathbf{x})$ is proportional to the negative minimum energy, $\text{s}_{\varphi}(\mathbf{x}) \propto -E(\mathbf{x})$. This mapping relies on converting the formula into a **Strict Conjunctive Normal Form (SDNF)**.

The LBM demonstrates robust capabilities across several domains. For **satisfiability (SAT)** problems, the system uses Gibbs sampling or free energy minimization to search for satisfying assignments. In **Maximum Satisfiability (MaxSAT)**, the goal of maximizing satisfied clauses ($\sum \mathbbm{1}(\mathbf{x}\models\varphi_m)$) is shown to be equivalent to minimizing the LBM's free energy function, $FE(\mathbf{x}) = \sum FE_m(\mathbf{x})$. While preliminary MaxSAT comparisons against the state-of-the-art solver **Loandra** on the **MaxCut** benchmark (6 instances) show LBM is comparable, the authors suggest stochastic global optimization methods like dual annealing are necessary for scalability.

In the realm of **Inductive Logic Programming (ILP)**, LBM is evaluated against symbolic systems like **Aleph [46]** and **CILP++ [13]** across seven datasets, including **Mutagenesis [47]**, **KRK [3]**, **UW-CSE [39]**, and the **Alzheimer’s** benchmark. The LBM achieves superior performance in **5 out of 7** datasets, demonstrating that integrating background knowledge (BK) significantly boosts learning accuracy.

Furthermore, the authors extend the framework to **first-order logic** for complex tasks like semantic image interpretation. By placing the LBM as a logical module atop feature extractors (e.g., **Faster-RCNN**), the system can infer relations like $\text{part}(\text{Screen},\text{TV})$ from visual features. This modular approach yields an **Area Under the Curve (AUC)** result that surpasses **Logic Tensor Networks (LTN)** in predicting the 'part-of' relation. The LBM's ability to provide a *provable* semantic guarantee makes it a key candidate for building verifiable, accountable AI systems, marking a significant step toward the "third wave" of AI.
