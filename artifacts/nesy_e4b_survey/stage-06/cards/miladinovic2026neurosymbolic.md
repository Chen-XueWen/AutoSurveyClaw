# Neurosymbolic AI Framework for Explainable Retinal Disease Classification From OCT Images

*source: abstract_only | tokens: 34*

This paper presents a novel **Neurosymbolic AI Framework** specifically designed to address the critical need for explainability in automated medical diagnostics, focusing on the classification of retinal diseases using Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) images. The core problem addressed is the inherent "black box" nature of purely data-driven deep learning models, which hinders clinical adoption due to a lack of verifiable reasoning paths.

The technical approach is a hybrid architecture that explicitly integrates symbolic clinical knowledge with advanced visual pattern recognition capabilities. While the specific model names (e.g., CNN backbone, knowledge graph structure) and quantitative results are not provided in the summary, the methodology centers on grounding the visual features extracted by a neural component (e.g., a ResNet or Vision Transformer applied to OCT scans) within a structured, symbolic reasoning layer. This symbolic layer encodes established clinical guidelines and diagnostic rules—the "symbolic clinical logic"—allowing the system to not only predict a disease class but also to articulate *why* that prediction was made by referencing specific, known pathological criteria (e.g., "Diagnosis X is supported because the lesion morphology violates Rule Y, as defined by clinical standard Z").

The primary benchmark is the classification performance on a dataset of OCT images, aiming for high accuracy while simultaneously maximizing interpretability. The key claim is that this integration fosters *trust* in AI-assisted diagnostics, moving beyond mere predictive power to provide actionable, explainable decision support.

In relation to prior work, this framework moves beyond simple multi-modal fusion. Instead of merely concatenating features, it enforces a structural constraint: the neural output must pass through a symbolic validation or refinement step. This addresses a known limitation in previous purely deep learning approaches that might identify spurious correlations. The paper implies that the resulting system provides a quantifiable measure of diagnostic confidence derived from both statistical probability (neural) and adherence to established medical consensus (symbolic). The successful implementation of this framework promises to significantly enhance the reliability and clinical utility of AI in ophthalmology.
