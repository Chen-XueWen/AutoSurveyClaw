# Neurosymbolic Reinforcement Learning and Planning: A Survey

*source: s2_fulltext | tokens: 16928*

Acharya et al. (2023) present a comprehensive survey on Neurosymbolic Reinforcement Learning (NSRL) in IEEE Transactions on Artificial Intelligence, addressing a critical gap in literature where Neurosymbolic AI and RL are typically surveyed separately. The authors propose a taxonomy classifying NSRL systems into three interaction models based on the role of neural and symbolic components: Learning for Reasoning (neural extracts symbols to narrow symbolic search space), Reasoning for Learning (symbolic knowledge guides neural policy learning and reward shaping), and Learning-Reasoning (bidirectional interaction for abstraction and regularization).

The survey analyzes specific architectures and benchmarks across diverse domains. In knowledge graph reasoning, it reviews DeepPath and MINERVA, noting their performance on Freebase and Never-Ending Language Learning datasets compared to path-ranking algorithms. For verification, VIPER and REVEL are highlighted for extracting interpretable models to ensure safety properties. In gaming, AlphaGo Zero is cited for achieving a 100-0 victory over its predecessor using self-play without human data. Other notable works include Deep Symbolic Reinforcement Learning (DSRL) for policy distillation, the DUA framework evaluated on the Animal-AI competition testbed, and MCTS-A for automaton-guided reward shaping in sparse reward environments.

Applications span robotics, question answering, and safe RL. The authors identify critical challenges hindering deployment: the automated generation of symbolic knowledge (logic rules remain largely manual and non-scalable), verification gaps in adversarial robustness, and the symbol grounding problem. They note that traditional RL algorithms often fail to account for the complexities of symbolic reasoning, necessitating new learning algorithms optimized for the union of both knowledge sets. They posit that future opportunities lie in causal RL integration and optimizing RL parameters via symbolic reasoning to handle sparse rewards. This work distinguishes itself from prior surveys (e.g., Yu et al., Besold et al.) by focusing exclusively on the RL intersection, offering a structured roadmap for developing interpretable and data-efficient agents. The survey concludes that while NSRL offers improved generalization and explainability, scalability remains a concern due to the complexity of aligning symbolic specifications with neural representations.
