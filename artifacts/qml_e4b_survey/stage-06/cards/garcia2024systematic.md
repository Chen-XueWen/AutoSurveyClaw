# Systematic literature review: Quantum machine learning and its applications

*source: arxiv_html | tokens: 20596*

García et al. (2023) present a systematic literature review (SLR) of Quantum Machine Learning (QML), analyzing 94 peer-reviewed articles published between 2017 and 2023. Following Kitchenham guidelines, the authors filtered 5497 initial records to 94 articles, categorizing algorithms into quantum implementations of classical methods (e.g., Support Vector Machines, K-Nearest Neighbors) and native quantum architectures (e.g., Quantum Neural Networks, Boltzmann Machines, Generative Adversarial Networks). The review identifies a strong prevalence of hybrid classical-quantum models, particularly Variational Quantum Classifiers (VQC) and Quantum Convolutional Neural Networks (QCNN), often utilizing parameterized quantum circuits (PQC) or ansatzes like IQP and StronglyEntanglingLayers. Specific algorithm families identified include Quantum Reservoir Computing (QRC), Quantum Boltzmann Machines (QBM), Quantum Monte Carlo, and Quantum Vision Transformers.

In terms of benchmarks, the surveyed literature heavily favors image classification tasks. Common datasets include MNIST, CIFAR-10, Fashion-MNIST, and smaller-scale problems like 2x2 or 4x4 pixel images due to qubit constraints. Other applications include natural language processing, time-series forecasting (NARMA task), and quantum-specific tasks like entanglement detection and error correction. Implementation environments are predominantly simulators (Qiskit, Cirq, TensorFlow Quantum) or early NISQ hardware from IBM (e.g., ibmqx2, ibmq_rome) and Rigetti.

Crucially for a survey on exponential advantage, the authors report that while QML shows promise, current results are largely "comparable" to classical counterparts rather than demonstrating exponential speedups. The study concludes that QML is "far from achieving its full potential" due to hardware limitations, specifically the lack of sufficient qubits, fault tolerance, and coherence times (T1/T2). Most successful implementations are limited to low-dimensional feature spaces (e.g., 2^N dimensional feature spaces via quantum kernels) but fail to outperform classical ML on complex tasks. The review highlights that quantum advantage remains theoretical for most applications, with current NISQ devices restricting performance to small-scale classification problems where classical models already achieve high accuracy. The authors note that while some hybrid approaches claim reduced training time, these are not consistently validated against classical baselines for complexity scaling. This underscores the gap between theoretical quantum speedups and practical NISQ-era implementations, suggesting that exponential advantage is currently unattainable without significant hardware improvements.
