# Exponential concentration and untrainability in quantum kernel methods

*source: arxiv_html | tokens: 52964*

Thanasilp et al. (2024) provide a critical theoretical analysis of the scalability of quantum kernel methods, challenging the prevailing assumption that convex optimization landscapes guarantee trainability and quantum advantage. The authors demonstrate that quantum kernels suffer from **exponential concentration**, where kernel values converge to a fixed constant as the number of qubits $n$ increases. This phenomenon renders the Gram matrix statistically indistinguishable from a random or identity matrix under polynomial measurement shots, causing the trained model to produce predictions independent of input data. Consequently, while training error remains low due to the convex landscape, generalization performance collapses.

The paper identifies four distinct mechanisms driving this concentration: (1) **Embedding Expressivity**, where highly expressive unitaries map data to random-like states; (2) **Entanglement**, where volume-law entangled states result in maximally mixed reduced states for projected kernels; (3) **Global Measurements**, where fidelity kernels require estimating exponentially small overlaps; and (4) **Noise**, where local Pauli noise drives states toward the maximally mixed state exponentially in circuit depth $L$. Analytical bounds are derived for each case, showing that the variance of kernel values scales as $\mathcal{O}(1/b^n)$, necessitating an exponential number of measurement shots to resolve data-dependent features.

Numerical simulations were conducted on a binary classification task using the **MNIST dataset** (distinguishing handwritten '0' and '1'), employing **Hardware Efficient Embeddings (HEE)** and tensor product embeddings. Results confirm that for $n \geq 30$, kernel estimates vanish or become random noise with polynomial shots. Crucially, the authors argue that standard **error mitigation** techniques (Zero-Noise Extrapolation, Virtual Distillation) cannot resolve this concentration barrier, as the underlying state indistinguishability persists. The work concludes that achieving quantum advantage with kernels requires problem-inspired embeddings that avoid these concentration sources, rather than problem-agnostic schemes. This finding serves as a significant caveat for the literature on exponential quantum advantage, suggesting that without careful embedding design, quantum kernel methods may fail to outperform classical counterparts in the NISQ era.
