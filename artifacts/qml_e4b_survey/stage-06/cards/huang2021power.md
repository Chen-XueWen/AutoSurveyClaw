# Power of data in quantum machine learning

*source: s2_fulltext | tokens: 10896*

Huang et al. (2021) critically assess the potential for exponential quantum advantage in supervised machine learning, arguing that the availability of training data significantly elevates classical computational power. The authors establish rigorous prediction error bounds for kernel-based methods, showing that classical models can learn quantum-generated functions efficiently if sufficient data is provided, even when simulating the underlying quantum circuit is classically hard. Central to their methodology is a "geometric difference" metric $g_{CQ}$, which measures the separation between classical and quantum kernel functions. This metric serves as a prescreen: if $g_{CQ}$ is small, classical ML is guaranteed competitive performance regardless of the target function's complexity. Theoretical analysis links prediction error to model complexity $s_K(N)$, demonstrating that for standard quantum kernels, the effective dimension $d$ of the state space often scales linearly with data size, limiting advantage.

In numerical experiments, the authors benchmarked standard Quantum Kernel (QK) methods against classical baselines including SVMs, Random Forests, and Neural Networks. Tests utilized the Fashion-MNIST dataset and custom engineered datasets designed to saturate the geometric inequality. Simulations scaled to 30 qubits, utilizing TensorFlow Quantum and achieving peak throughputs of 1.1 petaflop/s. Results indicated that standard QK methods frequently underperformed classical models because the effective dimension $d$ of the quantum state space grew exponentially with qubit count, causing kernel matrices to approach the identity and reducing $g_{CQ}$. To address this, the authors proposed Projected Quantum Kernels (PQK), which project quantum states onto lower-dimensional classical representations (e.g., using 1-RDMs or classical shadows). On engineered datasets, PQK demonstrated a prediction error advantage exceeding 20% over the best classical models up to 30 qubits. The work concludes that exponential advantage in QML is not guaranteed by quantum hardness alone; it requires specific data encodings that maintain large geometric differences. This provides a crucial filter for identifying viable QML applications, suggesting that many existing proposals lack advantage due to the power of data to simplify learning tasks. The authors note that while their constructed datasets are engineered, the methodology allows for generating verifiable problems that are hard for classical ML but easy for quantum devices, potentially enabling fault-tolerant advantage with modest qubit counts. They further suggest that this separation may persist under moderate noise, opening a path for near-term advantage claims that are robust to device imperfections.
