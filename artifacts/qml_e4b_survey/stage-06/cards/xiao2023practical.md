# Practical advantage of quantum machine learning in ghost imaging

*source: s2_fulltext | tokens: 13020*

Xiao et al. (2023) demonstrate practical quantum advantage in machine learning applied to ghost imaging (GI), a task characterized by blind object identification and reconstruction under low sampling rates. The authors address the NISQ-era challenge of identifying applications where quantum computation outperforms classical counterparts without relying on theoretical speedups alone. Their proposed hybrid quantum-classical framework utilizes parameterized quantum circuits (PQC) coupled with classical neural networks. A key technical contribution is a physical-inspired patch strategy that partitions high-dimensional bucket signals into independent segments, enabling parallel variational circuits to process data within the qubit constraints of current hardware. The study employs two experimental setups: one using bucket signals from the MNIST handwritten digit dataset for classification, and another using the RarePlanes remote sensing dataset for regression-based image reconstruction.

In the identification task, the QML model achieved a 3–10% improvement in validation accuracy over classical machine learning (CML) baselines with comparable parameter counts. This advantage persisted even under simulated decoherence rates of $10^{-3}$, though performance degraded at $3 \times 10^{-3}$. For blind imaging, QML demonstrated superior convergence; Mean Absolute Error (MAE) was reduced by factors of 3.6 to 5.9 compared to CML across sampling rates $M=64$ to $512$. Image quality metrics confirmed this, with QML achieving Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR) up to 8 dB higher than CML (23.24 dB vs. 15.12 dB at $M=512$) and consistently higher Structural Similarity (SSIM). The authors provide a theoretical justification for this advantage using information geometry, specifically calculating the Local Effective Dimension (LED). Their results show QML models maintain a lower LED, indicating better generalization capabilities and parameter efficiency in high-dimensional feature spaces. Unlike prior QML works that utilized pure classical datasets, this study emphasizes that QML is particularly suited for data generated from physical systems. By framing the imaging problem as an inverse physical process, the authors argue that the exponentially larger quantum feature Hilbert space allows for more effective learning of complex distributions. This work provides empirical evidence of practical quantum advantage in a physics-related application, suggesting that NISQ devices can outperform classical methods in specific, resource-constrained sensing tasks.
