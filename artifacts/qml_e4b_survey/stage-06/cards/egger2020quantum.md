# Quantum Computing for Finance: State-of-the-Art and Future Prospects

*source: s2_fulltext | tokens: 26747*

Egger et al. (2020) present a comprehensive survey on quantum computing applications in finance, published in *IEEE Transactions on Quantum Engineering*. The authors categorize financial challenges into three primary problem classes: simulation, optimization, and machine learning (ML), mapping specific quantum algorithms to each. While the paper covers broad financial use cases, it provides critical nuance regarding quantum advantage claims, particularly distinguishing between proven speedups and speculative ML benefits.

In the ML section (Section V), the survey focuses on supervised learning tasks, specifically binary classification for fraud detection and credit scoring. The authors evaluate Variational Quantum Classification (VQC) and Quantum Kernel Estimation (QKE) as methods to leverage quantum-enhanced feature spaces. Empirical demonstrations utilize a synthetic credit card transaction dataset (100 records) containing mixed continuous and categorical features. The study compares VQC against classical Support Vector Classifiers (SVC). A key technical contribution is the integration of Quantum Random Access Coding (QRAC) for discrete feature encoding (VQCwQRAC), which achieved accuracy comparable to classical RBF-kernel SVCs. However, the authors explicitly note that the potential quantum advantage for ML remains speculative; there is no theoretical proof that quantum feature maps guarantee superior accuracy over classical classifiers, and variational methods are heuristics prone to local optima.

Regarding computational advantage, the paper distinguishes between problem classes. For simulation (Section III), Quantum Amplitude Estimation (AE) is shown to offer a quadratic speedup over classical Monte Carlo methods ($O(1/M)$ convergence vs. $O(1/\sqrt{M})$), specifically demonstrated for Value-at-Risk (VaR) and Expected Capital Requirement (ECR) calculations. In contrast, for ML and optimization (Sections IV-V), the advantage relies on the intractability of the feature space or combinatorial complexity, but no exponential speedup is claimed for current NISQ implementations. The survey highlights significant technical bottlenecks, including the exponential cost of data loading ($O(4^k)$ for generic states) and the requirement for fault-tolerant quantum computers (FTQC) for deep circuits like AE.

This survey serves as a grounded reference for the "Exponential Quantum Advantage in Machine Learning" topic by tempering expectations. It confirms that while simulation tasks show proven quadratic speedups, ML applications currently lack empirical evidence of exponential advantage, with performance largely matching classical baselines on small-scale synthetic data.
