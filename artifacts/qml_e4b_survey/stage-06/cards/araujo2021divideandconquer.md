# A divide-and-conquer algorithm for quantum state preparation

*source: s2_fulltext | tokens: 8976*

Araujo et al. (2021) address the critical bottleneck of classical data loading in quantum machine learning, where standard amplitude encoding algorithms (Möttönen et al., 2005; Shende et al., 2006) require circuit depth $O(N)$, scaling exponentially with qubit count $n = \log_2 N$. The authors propose a divide-and-conquer state preparation algorithm that trades circuit width for depth. By utilizing $O(N)$ qubits (including entangled ancillary registers) and controlled-swap operations in a bottom-up strategy, the method achieves a quantum circuit depth of $O(\log_2^2 N)$, offering an exponential time advantage for large $N$. The encoding modifies the target state to $x_0|0\rangle|\psi_0\rangle + \dots + x_{N-1}|N-1\rangle|\psi_{N-1}\rangle$, where information is stored in probability amplitudes of orthonormal basis sets entangled with ancilla.

Experimental validation on the IBM Q ibmq_rome device confirmed the proof-of-concept for a 4-dimensional vector. Comparative benchmarks against standard amplitude encoding methods show a crossover in efficiency at data dimensions larger than 32. For $N=16$, the proposed method depth (58) is significantly lower than the non-optimized Möttönen implementation (277), though standard Shende implementation (47) remains competitive. The theoretical advantage becomes dominant as $N$ increases.

Two QML applications were evaluated: a hierarchical quantum classifier using Tree Tensor Networks (TTN) and a swap test for statistical analysis. The classifier was tested on four datasets: Iris, Haberman’s Survival, Banknote Authentication, and Pima Indians Diabetes. Results indicated classification accuracy statistically equivalent to standard amplitude encoding (Wilcoxon test, $\alpha=0.05$), with the advantage of lower circuit depth for $N \geq 64$. The swap test application demonstrated the ability to calculate covariance and variance metrics efficiently, reducing classical complexity from $O(Nm^2)$ to $O_q(\log_2^2(N)m^2)$ for multivariate variables.

Limitations include the linear qubit requirement ($O(N)$) compared to the logarithmic scaling of standard methods, and the necessity of entangled ancillary states which result in a mixed state if traced out. The authors suggest future work on uncomputing ancilla information. This work is pivotal for the "Exponential Quantum Advantage in Machine Learning" survey as it theoretically resolves the data loading bottleneck that often negates quantum speedups in practical QML pipelines, shifting the constraint from time to space.
