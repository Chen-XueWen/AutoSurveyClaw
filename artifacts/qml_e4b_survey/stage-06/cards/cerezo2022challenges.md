# Challenges and opportunities in quantum machine learning

*source: arxiv_pdf | tokens: 14727*

Cerezo et al. (2023) offer a comprehensive survey of Quantum Machine Learning (QML), analyzing the landscape of models, challenges, and opportunities for achieving quantum advantage. The authors categorize QML tasks based on data type—classical versus quantum—and algorithm type, covering key paradigms such as Parameterized Quantum Circuits (PQCs) used as Quantum Neural Networks (QNNs), Quantum Convolutional Neural Networks (QCNNs), and quantum kernel methods. A significant portion of the review addresses the "trainability" bottleneck, specifically the barren plateau phenomenon where gradient magnitudes vanish exponentially with qubit count (McClean et al., 2018; Cerezo et al., 2021). The survey highlights that hardware noise exacerbates this issue, creating noise-induced barren plateaus that effectively flatten the optimization landscape on NISQ devices.

In the context of exponential quantum advantage, the paper draws a sharp distinction between data types. For classical data (e.g., MNIST, Iris), the authors note that dequantization results (Tang, 2019) suggest no known exponential advantage, though quadratic speedups remain possible. Conversely, for quantum data, they cite theoretical proofs by Huang et al. (2021, 2022) establishing exponential separations in sample complexity, which are robust against dequantization. This theoretical advantage is supported by empirical demonstrations on the Sycamore processor. Promising application domains include quantum sensing, metrology, and the discovery of quantum error correction codes (QECCs), where QML can identify optimal measurements or subspaces in Hilbert space. The authors emphasize that realizing these advantages requires architectures with sharp inductive biases, such as QCNNs, to avoid barren plateaus. They further propose "Quantum Geometric Deep Learning" as a framework to encode physical symmetries into model priors. Ultimately, the survey posits that the transition to the fault-tolerant era will enable the storage and processing of native quantum data, making QML a natural framework for learning from quantum simulations in chemistry and materials science. The authors conclude that standardized quantum datasets are currently lacking, representing a critical gap that must be filled to benchmark models on true quantum data rather than classical proxies.
