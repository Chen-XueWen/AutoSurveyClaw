# Quantum-enhanced least-square support vector machine: Simplified quantum algorithm and sparse solutions

*source: s2_fulltext | tokens: 8506*

Lin et al. (2019) address critical limitations in existing quantum Least-Square Support Vector Machine (LS-SVM) algorithms, specifically the lack of solution sparsity and the high resource costs associated with matrix inversion. In the context of exponential quantum advantage in machine learning, the authors propose two distinct algorithms: the Hybrid Variable Quantum SVM (HVQ-SVM) and the Quantum Sparse LS-SVM (QSLS-SVM). These methods aim to retain the exponential speed-up over classical counterparts while improving feasibility for near-term quantum hardware.

The HVQ-SVM algorithm introduces a novel matrix inversion technique using continuous variable (CV) quantum states. Unlike previous approaches relying on standard phase estimation with many ancillary qubits, this method utilizes an integral form of matrix inversion assisted by CV states. The authors derive a time complexity of $O(\epsilon^{-1} \log(MN))$, where $M$ is the sample size and $N$ is the feature dimension. Notably, the paper demonstrates that the finite squeezing errors inherent in this CV method act as a natural $L_2$ regularization, improving generalization. Error analysis indicates that homodyne detection post-selection introduces fidelity issues that correlate with regularization strength.

The QSLS-SVM algorithm focuses on generating sparse solutions, a key advantage of classical SVMs often lost in quantum LS-SVM variants. This hybrid approach encodes the training data into a quantum state and employs a quantum matrix toolbox—specifically Quantum Principal Component Analysis (QPCA) or Quantum Singular-Value Thresholding (QSVT)—to compress the dataset. By extracting only the top-$R$ singular values, the algorithm reduces the number of support vectors required for inference, lowering classical post-processing costs. The complexity is analyzed as $O(N + R^3 + R(\epsilon^{-1} \log(MN)))$. Both algorithms support quantum feature maps, with explicit constructions provided for polynomial kernels (via tensor products) and Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernels (via approximation techniques).

While the work theoretically establishes exponential speed-up with respect to sample size $M$, it relies on the QRAM assumption for efficient state preparation and assumes low-rank matrices for optimal sparsity extraction. The authors acknowledge that for high-rank matrices, QSVT thresholding is required to eliminate small eigenvalues. This paper refines the theoretical landscape of quantum SVMs by integrating sparsity and resource efficiency, offering a more practical pathway to demonstrating exponential quantum advantage in classification tasks compared to prior work by Rebentrost et al. (2014).
