# Quantum machine learning of large datasets using randomized measurements

*source: arxiv_pdf | tokens: 15172*

Haug, Self, and Kim (2021) address the scalability bottleneck in quantum kernel machine learning, where conventional measurement methods scale quadratically with dataset size ($L^2$), rendering them impractical for large datasets on NISQ devices. They propose using randomized measurements to estimate quantum kernels, reducing quantum computational time to scale linearly with $L$, while classical post-processing remains $O(L^2)$. This yields a quadratic speedup in quantum resources compared to swap or inversion tests. For a dataset size of $L=10^3$, the method requires a factor of 100 fewer measurements than previous works. The authors characterize the encoding via the Quantum Fisher Information Metric (QFIM), showing that hardware-efficient circuits (specifically the Natural PQC with $N=8$ qubits and $d=4$ layers) generate kernels approximating radial basis functions (RBF) with the QFIM as a weight matrix.

Experimental validation was performed on IBM Quantum devices (ibmq guadalupe, ibmq toronto) using a handwritten digit dataset (1797 images, 8x8 pixels, 64 features). The quantum kernel-based SVM achieved classification accuracy comparable to classical RBF kernels. Crucially, the authors demonstrate a cost-free error mitigation scheme for global depolarizing noise, boosting experimental accuracy to near noiseless simulation levels. They estimate that processing the full MNIST training dataset ($L=60,000$) would require approximately 240 hours of quantum processing time with their scheme, compared to at least 10 years using inversion or swap tests.

Regarding exponential quantum advantage, the paper argues that while the method enables practical benchmarking on large datasets, the kernel structure resembles classical RBFs. The authors conclude that for this encoding, "these quantum kernels are unlikely to achieve an advantage over classical methods." Thus, the work serves as a critical boundary case for the survey: it establishes a pathway for large-scale QML but suggests that exponential advantage may not arise from kernel expressivity in this regime, instead relying on resource efficiency for NISQ devices. The study highlights that while quantum computation time scales linearly, the lack of distinct quantum feature space separation limits the potential for exponential speedup in classification tasks compared to classical counterparts. The error mitigation scheme, derived from measurement statistics, allows extraction of kernels even from noisy devices, though the underlying kernel similarity to classical methods remains a constraint on theoretical advantage.
