# A Preprocessing Perspective for Quantum Machine Learning Classification Advantage in Finance Using NISQ Algorithms

*source: arxiv_pdf | tokens: 10280*

Mancilla and Pere (2022) address the persistent challenge of demonstrating Quantum Machine Learning (QML) advantage on Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) devices, where limited qubit counts and noise hinder performance on high-dimensional classical data. Rather than claiming exponential speedup, the authors propose that practical classification advantage can be achieved through optimized classical preprocessing prior to quantum encoding. The study investigates the impact of dimensionality reduction techniques—specifically Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) versus Principal Component Analysis (PCA)—on Variational Quantum Classifiers (VQA) and Quantum Support Vector Classifiers (QSVC).

The methodology employs a hybrid pipeline where high-dimensional financial datasets are compressed to match the available qubit count (2 qubits in simulation) before encoding via ZZFeatureMap or angle encoding. The authors benchmarked against classical baselines including Logistic Regression, CART, KNN, and SVM on two imbalanced financial datasets: the UCI Default of Credit Card Clients (25 features, 30k rows) and a Fraud Detection dataset (114 features, 20k rows).

Results indicate that LDA preprocessing significantly outperforms PCA and other methods (SVD, SKPP) in enabling quantum classifiers to surpass classical baselines. On the UCI dataset, the VQA combined with LDA achieved a Balanced Accuracy (BA) of 92.54% and an F1-score of 58.46%, compared to the best classical baseline (CART) at 60.76% BA. Similarly, QSVC with LDA reached 64.6% BA. On the Fraud dataset, VQA+LDA achieved 83.92% BA, outperforming classical SVM and LR baselines. The authors argue that quantum advantage in this context is defined by superior information extraction via feature mapping rather than asymptotic runtime scaling, citing Schuld and Killoran (2022).

Limitations include the reliance on simulators (Qiskit Aer, Pennylane) without noise modeling, a fixed hyperparameter configuration (2 feature map repetitions), and a focus solely on binary classification in finance. For a survey on exponential advantage, this work is critical as it delineates a pathway to *practical* advantage in the NISQ era, suggesting that preprocessing strategies are a prerequisite for realizing quantum utility before theoretical exponential scaling becomes hardware-accessible.
