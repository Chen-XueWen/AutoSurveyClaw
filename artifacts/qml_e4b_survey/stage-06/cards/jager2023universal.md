# Universal expressiveness of variational quantum classifiers and quantum kernels for support vector machines

*source: s2_fulltext | tokens: 8251*

Jäger and Krems (2023) establish the theoretical universality of Variational Quantum Classifiers (VQC) and Quantum Kernel Support Vector Machines (QSVM) regarding quantum computational advantage. Published in *Nature Communications*, this theory paper addresses the fundamental question of whether standard QML architectures can solve problems in the complexity class BQP (Bounded-Error Quantum Polynomial-Time). The authors prove that both VQC and QSVM possess PROMISEBQP-complete expressive power, implying they can efficiently solve any classification problem derived from a BQP decision problem.

The technical approach centers on the k-FORRELATION problem, a PROMISEBQP-complete task introduced by Aaronson and Ambainis. The authors construct a specific classification dataset based on k Boolean functions, encoding instances into n-qubit quantum states via a feature map $U_\Phi(x)$. They utilize a multi-hot encoding scheme where each function depends on at most three input bits, reducing data dimensionality to linear in k and polynomial in n. They demonstrate that for odd k ≥ 3, the problem remains complete even with these restrictions, allowing implementation via fixed ansatz circuits of polynomial depth $O(kn^3)$. Crucially, they show that the VQC decision rule and the QSVM kernel function $k(x_i, x_j)$ can be configured to distinguish between positive and negative k-FORRELATION instances with arbitrary accuracy by estimating the amplitude of the all-zero state.

This work provides a stronger theoretical guarantee than prior complexity-based QML proofs, such as Liu et al. (2021), which relied on the Discrete Logarithm Problem (DLP) in NP. Because PROMISEBQP includes all BQP problems, the existence of a quantum advantage (assuming BQP ≠ BPP) implies that VQC and QSVM can achieve exponential speedups for specific classification tasks that are intractable for classical computers. The authors further demonstrate the equivalence of VQC and QSVM expressiveness in this context, challenging the notion that QSVM is strictly superior due to optimal measurements.

Limitations include the reliance on idealized noiseless simulations and the theoretical nature of the proof; practical deployment requires realizing specific feature maps on hardware. Nevertheless, the paper serves as a critical benchmark for QML theory, confirming that standard variational and kernel-based models are theoretically capable of capturing exponential quantum advantage if the underlying complexity assumptions hold. It bridges the gap between abstract complexity theory and practical QML model design, suggesting that with the correct feature map, standard QML models are not limited to polynomial speedups.
