# Universal Approximation Property of Quantum Machine Learning Models in Quantum-Enhanced Feature Spaces

*source: s2_fulltext | tokens: 15002*

Goto, Tran, and Nakajima (2021) theoretically establish the Universal Approximation Property (UAP) of Quantum Machine Learning (QML) models operating in quantum-enhanced feature spaces. Addressing the gap in understanding the expressive power of quantum feature maps on near-term devices, the authors define a "quantum feature framework" where classical data $x \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is encoded into quantum states via unitary circuits $V(x)$, and output functions are constructed as linear combinations of expectation values of observables.

The study analyzes two distinct encoding scenarios to determine the limits of this expressivity. In the **parallel scenario**, the feature map is a tensor product of $N$ subsystems, allowing the number of qubits to scale freely. In the **sequential scenario**, a single qubit is repeatedly acted upon by a fixed circuit, varying the depth rather than width. The authors prove that the parallel framework achieves UAP for any continuous function on a compact set $X \subset \mathbb{R}^d$, utilizing the Stone-Weierstrass theorem to show that the induced basis functions can generate any polynomial. They also demonstrate classification capability, proving the model can separate arbitrary disjoint regions. For the sequential scenario, UAP is proven for finite input sets under linear independence conditions on the encoding parameters (derived via the Kronecker-Weyl theorem).

A critical contribution is the quantification of the approximation rate. For Lipschitz continuous target functions, the parallel framework achieves an error decay of $\epsilon = O(d^{3/2}N^{-1})$ with $N$ qubits. This rate matches the best-known approximation rates for classical neural networks. Consequently, the authors conclude that QML models in feature spaces possess at least the same expressivity as classical schemes.

For a survey on exponential quantum advantage, this paper is pivotal for distinguishing *expressivity* from *computational speedup*. Since classical models can also approximate continuous functions efficiently, the mere existence of a quantum feature map does not guarantee an exponential advantage. The authors suggest that potential quantum speedups must arise from the classical intractability of estimating specific kernel matrices or data hardness assumptions (e.g., discrete logarithm), rather than from the feature space's ability to represent functions. This work thus sets a theoretical baseline: quantum advantage in ML requires more than just universal approximation; it requires specific structural advantages in the kernel or data encoding that are classically hard to simulate. This finding refines the search for quantum advantage, directing focus toward data-dependent hardness rather than model capacity.
