## Quantum Advantage in Machine Learning: A Systematic Taxonomy of Computational Bottlenecks ### Abstract The exponential potential of quantum computation promises transformative capabilities for solving computationally intractable problems, yet the field critically lacks a unified theoretical framework to delineate when and how this advantage manifests in machine learning. Current literature often treats Quantum Machine Learning (QML) monolithically, failing to structurally distinguish the true source of computational hardness. This survey introduces a systematic taxonomy organized by the *source of computational hardness*, rigorously dissecting QML into four distinct paradigms: feature space expansion, physics-informed data processing, generative modeling, and parameterized circuit optimization. We analyze the mathematical underpinnings supporting each approach, revealing that the most robust theoretical advantage arises from problems constrained by fundamental physical laws, such as simulating quantum dynamics using Hamiltonians [kuhne2020electronic]. Furthermore, we systematically compare the resource scaling challenges inherent in quantum kernel estimation versus variational circuits, noting that the practical difficulty of robust training often masks the theoretical potential. The synthesis reveals that successful advancement requires moving beyond classical data proxies toward quantum-native data sources, pointing toward potential speedups that scale super-polynomially with system size for specific Hamiltonian evolution tasks. This taxonomy provides a crucial structural guide for future research, directing the community toward developing standardized benchmarks that rigorously test the boundary between quantum-assisted and classically superior computation. ### Introduction The ability to process information exponentially faster than classical computers represents a paradigm shift, with quantum computation leading the theoretical charge for such unprecedented computational capabilities. This potential is most vividly explored within Quantum Machine Learning (QML), an interdisciplinary field dedicated to harnessing quantum principles—such as superposition and entanglement—to enhance the capabilities of classical machine learning paradigms [zeguendry2023quantum]. The fundamental promise centers on achieving an exponential quantum advantage, meaning that for certain classes of problems, the time or memory resources required by a quantum processor scale polynomially, while the best known classical counterpart scales exponentially [egger2020quantum]. This theoretical possibility suggests that QML could unlock solutions in fields ranging from materials discovery to complex financial modeling, fundamentally altering computational science [barca2020recent]. A critical complication arises from the sheer methodological diversity within the literature, which currently obscures a clear path toward demonstrable advantage. While seminal works have explored quantum support vector machines [cervantes2020comprehensive] or quantum neural networks [chakraborty2020analytical], the body of research frequently organizes itself around the *algorithm* employed—be it a Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) or a Quantum Kernel Estimator—rather than based on the fundamental *source* of the computational difficulty itself. Consequently, assessing the true potential advantage is hampered by an inability to systematically distinguish between an inherent quantum speedup and merely an algorithmic novelty applied to a limited, non-representative dataset [raubitzek2023applicability]. This structural ambiguity necessitates a high-level, structural synthesis that transcends mere algorithmic cataloging to map the underlying mathematical barriers. To address this ambiguity, this survey proposes a rigorous taxonomy rooted in the **Source of Computational Hardness**.
