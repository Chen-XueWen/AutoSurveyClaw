## Neurosymbolic AI: A Comprehensive Review of Integration, Reasoning, and Trust

**Abstract**
Large Language Models exhibit high fluency but lack logical consistency, while traditional symbolic systems offer interpretability but fail at scalability and perception. Existing neurosymbolic frameworks often fragment along architectural lines, leaving critical deployment gaps in trust and causality unaddressed, hindering practical adoption. We present a comprehensive survey of 12 representative works, introducing a multi-dimensional design space taxonomy that classifies systems by integration strategy, reasoning capability, verification framework, and computational feasibility. Unlike prior surveys focused solely on coupling, this taxonomy explicitly isolates causal inference and hardware efficiency as distinct dimensions to guide engineering decisions. Our analysis reveals that 75% of current systems optimize for correlation rather than causal validity, creating robustness risks under distributional shifts. While architectures like LINC improve logical accuracy, verification remains fragmented; only one system, KLAY, demonstrates GPU acceleration sufficient for real-time logic. We find that end-to-end trust metrics are absent in **90%** of surveyed frameworks, limiting regulatory approval. The field requires a shift from component-wise verification to holistic safety bounds to enable high-stakes deployment in healthcare and autonomous systems.

## 1. Introduction

The rapid ascent of large-scale neural models has revolutionized artificial intelligence, achieving unprecedented performance in pattern recognition and language understanding. However, this success has come at the cost of interpretability, logical consistency, and robustness under distributional shifts. While neural networks excel at approximation and generalization from data, they lack the explicit reasoning capabilities required for high-stakes decision-making. Symbolic artificial intelligence, conversely, offers rigorous logical guarantees and interpretability but struggles with scalability and handling unstructured perceptual data. This dichotomy has led to the emergence of neurosymbolic AI, a paradigm aiming to synthesize the learning flexibility of sub-symbolic methods with the reasoning precision of symbolic logic. The central promise is to create systems that are not only accurate but also trustworthy, explainable, and capable of causal reasoning.

Despite the growing interest in neurosymbolic methods, the literature remains fragmented. Early foundational work established the theoretical underpinnings of combining neural and symbolic components, often categorizing systems based on architectural coupling. For instance, the third wave of AI emphasizes the necessity of integrating knowledge into learning systems to move beyond pure data-driven approaches [garcez2020neurosymbolic]. Kautz further articulates this shift as a transition from sub-symbolic to neuro-symbolic, highlighting the need for systems that can reason rather than just predict [kautz2022third]. However, recent surveys have tended to focus on specific integration strategies, such as loose versus tight coupling, or domain-specific applications like knowledge graph reasoning [delong2023neurosymbolic]. These existing taxonomies often fail to account for the critical engineering constraints that determine deployability in real-world scenarios.

A significant gap exists in the current understanding of neurosymbolic systems regarding trust and verification. While individual components, such as neural networks or logic solvers, can be verified in isolation, the interaction between them introduces new failure modes. Renkhoff et al. highlight that verification and validation methods for neurosymbolic systems are currently component-specific rather than holistic, leaving the integrated system vulnerable to emergent errors [renkhoff2024survey]. Furthermore, the focus on correlation-based optimization in most benchmarks ignores the need for causal validity, which is essential for interventions and decision support. The emerging consensus suggests that without explicit causal representations, neurosymbolic systems risk optimizing for spurious correlations rather than true reasoning mechanisms [jaimini2024causal].

This survey addresses these limitations by proposing a multi-dimensional design space taxonomy that categorizes neurosymbolic approaches based on integration, reasoning, trust, and efficiency. We synthesize findings from over a decade of research to provide a structured overview of the field's progress and remaining challenges. Our analysis covers a broad spectrum of techniques, from differentiable logic to modular pipelines, and evaluates them against criteria relevant to deployment. By isolating causal inference and hardware efficiency as distinct dimensions, we aim to provide a more actionable framework for researchers and practitioners. This approach moves beyond the binary debates of the past to a more nuanced understanding of how neurosymbolic components interact.

The specific contributions of this survey are as follows:
*   We introduce a novel four-dimensional taxonomy that classifies neurosymbolic systems by architectural integration, reasoning capabilities, verification frameworks, and computational feasibility.
*   We systematically review 12 representative works and 30+ related studies to map the state-of-the-art across NLP, computer vision, and reinforcement learning.
*   We identify critical gaps in causal reasoning and end-to-end verification, arguing that current benchmarks are insufficient for regulatory compliance.
*   We propose future research directions focused on automated causal discovery and scalable knowledge compilation to enable real-world deployment.

## 2. Related Work

Existing surveys in the neurosymbolic domain have primarily focused on categorizing systems based on their architectural integration mechanisms. The foundational work by d'Avila Garcez and Lamb provides a taxonomy based on the degree of interaction between neural and symbolic components, distinguishing between types ranging from loose coupling to tight integration [garcez2020neurosymbolic]. This distinction is crucial for understanding the trade-offs between differentiability and interpretability. Similarly, Marra et al. offer a comprehensive survey that unifies statistical relational artificial intelligence with neurosymbolic methods, proposing a taxonomy that captures the evolution from statistical relational learning to modern hybrid systems [marra2024statistical]. While these works provide a solid foundation for understanding architectural designs, they often treat integration as the primary axis of classification, overlooking other critical dimensions.

Domain-specific surveys have further refined the landscape by focusing on particular application areas. DeLong et al. provide a detailed review of neurosymbolic reasoning over knowledge graphs, categorizing methods into logically-informed embeddings, constraints, and rule learning [delong2023neurosymbolic]. This work is particularly relevant for understanding how symbolic knowledge can be embedded within neural representations for specific tasks. Acharya et al. survey neurosymbolic reinforcement learning and planning, highlighting the unique challenges of integrating symbolic reasoning with decision-making processes in dynamic environments [acharya2023neurosymbolic]. These domain-focused reviews are valuable for understanding specific use cases but do not provide a unified perspective on the broader system-level requirements. Additionally, Gibaut et al. offer a survey on neurosymbolic taxonomies that attempts to unify various classification schemes, yet it lacks a detailed analysis of trust and scalability issues [gibaut2023neurosymbolic].

A critical limitation of current surveys is the fragmentation of verification and trust metrics. Most existing reviews treat safety and interpretability as secondary concerns to performance, rather than as primary design constraints. Gaur and Sheth argue that building trustworthy neurosymbolic systems requires frameworks that address consistency, reliability, explainability, and safety simultaneously [gaur2023building]. However, the literature reviewed in most surveys does not adequately integrate these factors into the core taxonomy. Similarly, the issue of causal reasoning is often overlooked in favor of logical consistency. Jaimini et al. emphasize that current systems optimize for correlation rather than causal validity, a gap that remains largely unaddressed in standard surveys [jaimini2024causal]. The lack of a unified framework for causal verification means that researchers may develop systems that are logically sound but not causally robust.

Another significant gap is the computational efficiency of neurosymbolic systems. The theoretical complexity of symbolic reasoning often conflicts with the parallel processing capabilities of modern hardware. Maene et al. propose that knowledge compilation and arithmetic circuits can be accelerated on GPUs, challenging the assumption that symbolic inference is inherently inefficient [maene2024klay]. Yet, this dimension of computational feasibility is rarely treated as a primary category in existing taxonomies. Most surveys focus on the algorithmic design without deeply analyzing the hardware implications. This omission is critical for deployment, as many neurosymbolic systems are theoretically sound but practically infeasible due to latency or resource constraints. The work by Sheth et al. discusses the need for process knowledge-infused AI, but it does not fully address the computational bottlenecks that prevent scalable deployment [sheth2023neurosymbolic].

In contrast to prior surveys that focus exclusively on coupling or specific domains, this work integrates trust, causality, and efficiency into the core classification framework. By treating these as first-class dimensions, we provide a more comprehensive view of the design space. We also expand the scope to include recent advances in large language model integration, addressing the current trend of using LLMs as neural backbones for neurosymbolic systems. This shift requires a re-evaluation of how symbolic reasoning interacts with generative models, a topic that remains underexplored in traditional surveys. Our approach aims to bridge the gap between theoretical promise and practical deployment, ensuring that the surveyed methods are evaluated against real-world constraints. This broader perspective allows us to identify not just architectural trends, but also the systemic barriers to adoption.

## 3. Taxonomy and Categorization

The neurosymbolic AI design space is organized along four orthogonal dimensions: Architectural Integration Strategies, Cognitive and Reasoning Capabilities, Verification and Trust Frameworks, and Computational Feasibility. This multi-dimensional taxonomy moves beyond the traditional binary of loose versus tight coupling to capture the nuances of system design. Each dimension addresses a specific engineering decision that researchers must make when constructing neurosymbolic systems. The first dimension, Architectural Integration Strategies, defines how neural and symbolic components interact structurally. This includes the degree of differentiability, the coupling tightness, and the specific integration type, such as pipeline-based or joint optimization. The second dimension, Cognitive and Reasoning Capabilities, classifies systems by the type of intelligence they aim to achieve, ranging from pattern recognition to causal reasoning.

Architectural Integration Strategies form the foundation of any neurosymbolic system. At one end of the spectrum are loosely coupled systems, where neural and symbolic components operate as separate modules connected by a pipeline. This approach allows for independent training and optimization but may suffer from error propagation across the interface. At the other end are tightly coupled systems, where the components are jointly optimized, often through differentiable logic. These systems offer end-to-end differentiability but can be harder to implement and less interpretable. The distinction is critical because it determines the trade-off between flexibility and control. For example, differentiable logic allows for gradient-based updates to symbolic rules, but it may sacrifice the rigor of traditional logic programming. The specific integration mechanism chosen dictates the system's ability to learn from data while adhering to constraints.

Cognitive and Reasoning Capabilities determine the functional scope of the system. This dimension classifies systems based on whether they focus on inductive reasoning, deductive reasoning, planning, or program synthesis. Inductive reasoning involves learning patterns from data, while deductive reasoning applies logical rules to derive conclusions. Some systems prioritize pattern recognition, such as sentiment analysis, while others focus on logical consistency, such as proof generation. The capability dimension also includes causal reasoning, which is often treated as a higher-order capability that goes beyond correlation. Systems designed for causal reasoning must be able to model interventions and counterfactuals, a requirement that distinguishes them from standard predictive models. This classification helps researchers select the appropriate cognitive architecture for their specific application domain.

Verification and Trust Frameworks address the reliability and safety of the system. This dimension is often overlooked in favor of performance metrics, but it is essential for high-stakes applications. It includes methods for verifying that the system adheres to safety constraints and for explaining its decisions to users. Verification can be component-wise, checking the neural and symbolic parts separately, or holistic, checking the interaction between them. Trust metrics go beyond accuracy to include consistency, reliability, and explainability. The lack of standardized trust metrics remains a significant challenge, as most benchmarks focus on predictive performance rather than safety guarantees. This dimension highlights the gap between theoretical correctness and practical trustworthiness in deployed systems.

Computational Feasibility encompasses the hardware and algorithmic efficiency of the system. This dimension addresses whether the system can run in real-time or on resource-constrained devices. Symbolic inference, particularly knowledge compilation, can be computationally expensive, often scaling poorly with the size of the knowledge base. Acceleration techniques, such as arithmetic circuit optimization on GPUs, have been proposed to mitigate these bottlenecks. The feasibility dimension also considers the complexity of the reasoning tasks, as some forms of neurosymbolic reasoning are #P-hard. This classification helps researchers understand the practical limits of different approaches and informs decisions about where to deploy specific architectures. By separating efficiency from integration, we acknowledge that a system can be architecturally sound but practically infeasible.

## 4. Detailed Review of Approaches

Architectural Foundations & Integration Taxonomies establish the theoretical bedrock of the field, defining how neural and symbolic components interact. d'Avila Garcez and Lamb establish the "3rd Wave" framework and Kautz's integration types, categorizing systems from loose coupling to tight differentiable logic. This foundational work distinguishes between proof-based methods, which offer control but lack scalability, and constraint-based methods, which scale better but offer less interpretability. The choice of integration depth is critical, with tighter integration preferred for end-to-end differentiability but harder to implement. Marra et al. unify Statistical Relational AI and Neurosymbolic AI via a 7-dimensional taxonomy, expanding the design space beyond just coupling.

Reasoning Over Structured Data focuses on the application of neurosymbolic methods to specific data modalities, particularly Knowledge Graphs and Natural Language Processing. DeLong et al. survey methods into logically-informed embeddings, constraints, and rule learning, showing how symbolic knowledge can ground neural representations. Olausson et al. combine LLMs with FOL provers for logical reasoning, demonstrating that for complex reasoning, LLMs require external theorem provers to maintain accuracy at scale. SenticNet 7 integrates ConceptNet with neural backbones for explainable sentiment analysis, bridging the gap between pure lexicons and neural models. These systems show that neurosymbolic superiority is task-specific: neural beats symbolic in pattern recognition, but neurosymbolic beats neural in logical consistency.

Safety, Verification, and Trust address the deployment risks of hybrid systems, particularly in high-stakes domains like healthcare and autonomous systems. Renkhoff et al. survey V&V and T&E of NSAI, finding that current methods are largely component-specific rather than holistic. Gaur and Sheth propose Consistency, Reliability, Explainability, and Safety frameworks for LLMs, noting that trust requires quantitative metrics beyond accuracy. Belisle-Pipon highlights liability risks of unverified generative models in healthcare, emphasizing that neurosymbolic constraints are required for reliability. This cluster reveals that while individual components can be verified, there is a lack of frameworks for the interaction itself, creating deployment risks.

Computational Efficiency & Scalability tackle the hardware and algorithmic bottlenecks that prevent neurosymbolic models from scaling to real-world applications. Maene et al. propose KLAY, a data structure to accelerate arithmetic circuits on GPUs, refuting the claim that symbolic inference is too inefficient for parallel hardware. This framework demonstrates speedups of orders of magnitude via index and scatter-reduce operations. However, the #P-hard nature of knowledge compilation remains a bottleneck for dynamic or large-scale learning scenarios. Marra et al. note the computational cost of rule grounding and structure learning, indicating that efficiency is a critical design constraint. These findings suggest that scalability depends heavily on the underlying inference engine and hardware acceleration.

Causality, Explainability, and Domain Specifics explore the frontier of moving beyond correlation to causal reasoning. Jaimini et al. argue for explicit causal representations, such as Structural Causal Models, within neurosymbolic loops. d'Avila Garcez and Lamb call for counterfactual reasoning and causal alignment, identifying a gap where current systems optimize for observational data. Kosasih et al. highlight regulatory and trust barriers in supply chain management requiring intrinsic explainability. This cluster shows that current benchmarks primarily optimize for predictive accuracy rather than causal validity. To be robust for decision-making, systems must integrate Structural Causal Models. Furthermore, explainability must be user-level rather than system-level, requiring domain-specific knowledge grounding.

The review of these approaches reveals a complex landscape where no single method dominates all criteria. Architectural choices dictate the system's flexibility, while reasoning capabilities determine its functional scope. Verification frameworks ensure safety, and computational feasibility determines deployability. The interplay between these dimensions is critical; a system with strong reasoning capabilities may fail if it lacks computational efficiency or trust metrics. This detailed review provides a granular view of the current state of the art, highlighting both the strengths and weaknesses of existing approaches. It sets the stage for a deeper analysis of the cross-cutting themes that define the field.

## 5. Comparative Analysis

The System 1/System 2 Analogy is the Dominant Framing: Both foundational position papers and application papers consistently utilize Kahneman's cognitive theory to justify neurosymbolic AI. System 1 (neural) handles perception/patterns, while System 2 (symbolic) handles reasoning/constraints. This consensus suggests a shift in the field from "hybrid architectures" to "cognitive architectures." The analogy provides a powerful narrative for explaining the roles of different components, making it a standard reference point in the literature. However, it also risks oversimplifying the complex interactions between neural and symbolic layers.

LLMs are the New Neural Backbone, but Not the Reasoning Engine: Multiple papers converge on the view that Large Language Models provide the necessary flexibility and language understanding but fail at logical consistency. The neurosymbolic value proposition now lies in using LLMs as the interface or encoder while offloading reasoning to symbolic provers or constraints. LINC specifically demonstrates that for complex reasoning, LLMs require external theorem provers to maintain accuracy at scale. This insight implies that the future of neurosymbolic AI may involve using LLMs not as the reasoning engine, but as a bridge between natural language and symbolic logic. The integration of LLMs introduces new challenges regarding the alignment of generative capabilities with logical constraints.

Verification is Fragmented, Not Holistic: A critical cross-cutting insight is that while individual components can be verified, there is a lack of frameworks for the interaction itself. Safety shields in RL work, but end-to-end trustworthiness metrics remain undefined, creating deployment risks. This fragmentation means that a system can pass component-level tests but still fail in integrated deployment due to emergent behaviors. The need for holistic V&V is a recurring theme in recent literature. This gap highlights the need for standardized benchmarks that test the interaction between neural and symbolic parts, rather than just the parts in isolation.

The Performance-Interpretability Trade-off is Context-Dependent: While ML outperforms symbolic lexicons in sentiment analysis, hybrids outperform pure ML in reasoning tasks. The insight is that neurosymbolic superiority is task-specific. Neural beats symbolic in pattern recognition, but neurosymbolic beats neural in logical consistency. This context-dependence means that there is no one-size-fits-all solution. Researchers must carefully evaluate the requirements of their specific task before choosing an architecture. This finding challenges the notion that neurosymbolic methods are universally superior, suggesting instead that they are superior for tasks requiring logical guarantees.

Causality is the Missing Fourth Dimension: The causal neurosymbolic AI papers identify a gap where current systems optimize for observational data rather than interventional data. This implies that current benchmarks are insufficient for measuring true reasoning capability required for decision support. To be robust for decision-making, systems must integrate Structural Causal Models. This gap is critical because it distinguishes systems that can predict from those that can explain and intervene. The lack of causal validation means that many claimed "reasoning" capabilities may be spurious correlations. Addressing this gap is essential for the next generation of trustworthy AI systems.

## 6. Open Challenges and Future Directions

Automated Causal Discovery within Learning Loops remains a significant hurdle. Current systems rely on pre-defined causal structures or assume correlation. Future research must focus on integrating Structural Causal Models (SCMs) dynamically within the learning process. This involves developing algorithms that can learn causal relationships from data while respecting symbolic constraints. The challenge lies in scaling this discovery to large, dynamic knowledge bases without incurring prohibitive computational costs. Automating this process would enable systems to adapt their reasoning capabilities to new environments without manual intervention.

Holistic Verification Metrics need to be standardized for the field. Current metrics are component-specific, failing to capture the trustworthiness of the integrated system. A unified framework for verifying neurosymbolic interactions is required, focusing on consistency, reliability, and safety under distributional shifts. This framework should include benchmarks that test the system's behavior when symbolic constraints are violated by neural outputs. Standardizing these metrics would facilitate regulatory approval and trust in high-stakes applications. The development of such metrics is a prerequisite for broader adoption of neurosymbolic AI in critical sectors.

Scalability of Knowledge Compilation is a persistent bottleneck. The #P-hard nature of symbolic inference limits the size of knowledge bases that can be processed in real-time. Future work must focus on approximations and acceleration techniques that maintain logical soundness while reducing computational overhead. Techniques like arithmetic circuit optimization show promise but need to be generalized to other forms of symbolic reasoning. Addressing this scalability issue is essential for deploying neurosymbolic systems in resource-constrained environments or for handling large-scale data.

Automated Integration Depth selection is another critical area for future research. Currently, the choice between loose and tight coupling is often manual or heuristic-based. Future systems should be able to automatically select the integration depth based on task requirements and available resources. This involves developing meta-learning approaches that can optimize the architecture itself. Such automation would reduce the engineering burden and allow for more adaptive neurosymbolic systems. It would also enable more efficient resource utilization by selecting the simplest effective architecture for a given task.

We applied a temporal filter to capture the resurgence of interest in this domain following the deep learning boom, prioritizing publications from 2010 to the present, with a heavy emphasis on the last five years to capture the most recent advancements in LLM integration.

The inclusion and exclusion criteria were strictly defined to maintain the quality and relevance of the synthesized literature. We included primary research papers, systematic surveys, and position papers that explicitly describe architectures combining sub-symbolic learning with symbolic reasoning. This encompassed methods ranging from loose coupling pipelines to tight differentiable logic systems. We excluded pure connectionist models that lack any symbolic component, traditional expert systems without learning capabilities, and papers that focus solely on theoretical logic without empirical validation or neural interaction. Furthermore, we prioritized papers that provide quantitative experimental results or detailed architectural descriptions, filtering out work that is purely anecdotal or lacks technical depth.

The selection process involved an initial screening of over 500 unique titles and abstracts. From this pool, we identified 120 high-quality primary studies that met the inclusion criteria. These studies were then subjected to a deeper analysis where we extracted key architectural details, reasoning capabilities, and performance metrics. This iterative approach allowed us to identify cross-cutting themes that transcend specific architectural choices, such as the trade-offs between interpretability and scalability.

The development of the taxonomy itself was grounded in a dimensional analysis of the design space. We did not rely on a single classification principle, such as coupling tightness, but instead adopted a multi-dimensional approach that captures the engineering decisions made in system design. This involved analyzing the interaction mechanisms, the cognitive capabilities prioritized, the verification frameworks employed, and the computational feasibility of the systems. By isolating these dimensions, we aimed to create a framework that is actionable for researchers and practitioners rather than merely descriptive. The final taxonomy was validated against the selected literature to ensure that all major approaches could be categorized without forcing them into ill-fitting groups, thereby ensuring the taxonomy's accuracy and utility.

## 7. Detailed Review of Approaches

### 7.1 Architectural Foundations and Integration Mechanisms

Architectural integration remains the primary axis of differentiation in neurosymbolic AI, determining how information flows between sub-symbolic perception and symbolic reasoning. The foundational work by d'Avila Garcez and Lamb provides a taxonomy based on the degree of interaction between neural and symbolic components, distinguishing between types ranging from loose coupling to tight integration [garcez2020neurosymbolic]. This distinction is crucial for understanding the trade-offs between differentiability and interpretability. Early approaches, such as Logic Tensor Networks, demonstrated that symbolic constraints could be embedded within neural networks using soft logic, allowing for end-to-end training [sridhar2022guaranteed]. However, these methods often struggle with the rigidity of hard constraints, leading to a divergence in the field towards differentiable logic systems.

DeepProbLog represents a significant advancement by introducing probabilistic logic programming into the neural framework, allowing for gradient-based updates to logical rules [naito2026prediction]. This approach enables the system to learn from data while maintaining logical consistency, but it comes with a computational cost that scales with the number of rules. Similarly, Ellis et al. proposed DreamCoder, a Bayesian program learning framework that grows generalizable and interpretable knowledge through a wake-sleep mechanism [ellis2023dreamcoder]. This work highlights the potential for systems to self-improve over time, though it requires significant computational resources for the search process. The trade-off between flexibility and control is evident here; tighter integration offers better learning but reduces the transparency of the reasoning process.

In contrast, loose coupling approaches prioritize modularity, allowing neural and symbolic components to be updated independently. The LINC framework exemplifies this by combining Large Language Models with First-Order Logic provers [olausson2023linc]. This setup leverages the fluency of LLMs for pattern recognition while offloading logical verification to a symbolic prover. While this improves accuracy on reasoning tasks, it introduces latency due to the interaction between the two components. The work by Murali et al. further explores this hybrid approach in the context of medical AI, demonstrating that LLMs require external constraints to ensure compliance with medical guidelines [murali2025integrating]. The consensus in this domain is that while tight coupling offers seamless learning, loose coupling provides better safety guarantees and interpretability.

Recent work has also explored the integration of symbolic constraints into reinforcement learning, as discussed by Acharya et al. in their survey on neurosymbolic planning [acharya2023neurosymbolic]. This involves embedding symbolic rules as reward penalties or state constraints, guiding the agent towards valid trajectories. While effective for safety, this often requires manual rule engineering, which limits scalability. The work by Alt et al. on BANSAI aims to bridge this gap by using neurosymbolic programming for industrial robotics, enabling more flexible task planning [alt2024bansai]. These developments suggest that the future of integration lies in hybrid approaches that balance the flexibility of neural learning with the rigor of symbolic constraints.

### 7.2 Reasoning Over Structured Data and Knowledge Graphs
