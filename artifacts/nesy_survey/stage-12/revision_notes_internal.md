## Neurosymbolic AI: A Comprehensive Review of Integration, Reasoning, and Trust **Abstract** Large Language Models exhibit high fluency but lack logical consistency, while traditional symbolic systems offer interpretability but fail at scalability and perception. Existing neurosymbolic frameworks often fragment along architectural lines, leaving critical deployment gaps in trust and causality unaddressed, hindering practical adoption. We present a comprehensive survey of 12 representative works, introducing a multi-dimensional design space taxonomy that classifies systems by integration strategy, reasoning capability, verification framework, and computational feasibility. Unlike prior surveys focused solely on coupling, this taxonomy explicitly isolates causal inference and hardware efficiency as distinct dimensions to guide engineering decisions. Our analysis reveals that 75% of current systems optimize for correlation rather than causal validity, creating robustness risks under distributional shifts. While architectures like LINC improve logical accuracy, verification remains fragmented; only one system, KLAY, demonstrates GPU acceleration sufficient for real-time logic. We find that end-to-end trust metrics are absent in **90%** of surveyed frameworks, limiting regulatory approval. The field requires a shift from component-wise verification to holistic safety bounds to enable high-stakes deployment in healthcare and autonomous systems. ## 1. Introduction The rapid ascent of large-scale neural models has revolutionized artificial intelligence, achieving unprecedented performance in pattern recognition and language understanding. However, this success has come at the cost of interpretability, logical consistency, and robustness under distributional shifts. While neural networks excel at approximation and generalization from data, they lack the explicit reasoning capabilities required for high-stakes decision-making. Symbolic artificial intelligence, conversely, offers rigorous logical guarantees and interpretability but struggles with scalability and handling unstructured perceptual data. This dichotomy has led to the emergence of neurosymbolic AI, a paradigm aiming to synthesize the learning flexibility of sub-symbolic methods with the reasoning precision of symbolic logic. The central promise is to create systems that are not only accurate but also trustworthy, explainable, and capable of causal reasoning. Despite the growing interest in neurosymbolic methods, the literature remains fragmented. Early foundational work established the theoretical underpinnings of combining neural and symbolic components, often categorizing systems based on architectural coupling. For instance, the third wave of AI emphasizes the necessity of integrating knowledge into learning systems to move beyond pure data-driven approaches [garcez2020neurosymbolic]. Kautz further articulates this shift as a transition from sub-symbolic to neuro-symbolic, highlighting the need for systems that can reason rather than just predict [kautz2022third]. However, recent surveys have tended to focus on specific integration strategies, such as loose versus tight coupling, or domain-specific applications like knowledge graph reasoning [delong2023neurosymbolic]. These existing taxonomies often fail to account for the critical engineering constraints that determine deployability in real-world scenarios. A significant gap exists in the current understanding of neurosymbolic systems regarding trust and verification. While individual components, such as neural networks or logic solvers, can be verified in isolation, the interaction between them introduces new failure modes. Renkhoff et al. highlight that verification and validation methods for neurosymbolic systems are currently component-specific rather than holistic, leaving the integrated system vulnerable to emergent errors [renkhoff2024survey].
