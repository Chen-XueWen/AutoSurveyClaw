# Building Trustworthy NeuroSymbolic AI Systems: Consistency, Reliability, Explainability, and Safety

*source: arxiv_html | tokens: 13633*

Gaur and Sheth (2023) argue that current Large Language Models (LLMs) lack inherent trustworthiness despite scaling, necessitating a Neurosymbolic AI (NeSy) approach for critical applications like healthcare. The authors identify three core failures in probabilistic LLMs: inconsistency (e.g., ChatGPT yielding different confidence levels on semantically similar gendered queries), safety vulnerabilities (e.g., GPT-3 advising self-harm), and a lack of user-level explainability. They propose the CREST framework—Consistency, Reliability, Explainability, and Safety—as a solution grounded in NeSy methods that fuse neural networks with symbolic knowledge graphs (KGs) and clinical practice guidelines.

The CREST framework introduces several technical innovations. For consistency, it employs NeSy-based paraphrasing using KGs like CommonSense and WordNet to ensure robustness against adversarial perturbations. For reliability, it proposes knowledge-infused ensembling (KnowLLMs) where external knowledge acts as a reward function during fine-tuning, rather than simple statistical averaging. Explainability is addressed through User-level Explainable Models (UExMs) that pair generators with evaluators grounded in domain concepts (e.g., PHQ-9 for depression). This "generative evaluator pairing" allows the system to penalize generic responses and reward contextually relevant follow-up questions, effectively guiding the LLM toward clinically actionable advice rather than hallucinated safety guidelines. Safety is achieved by grounding generation in expert-defined rules, such as National Institute on Drug Abuse quizzes, to prevent misalignment.

Empirical validation was conducted on the PRIMATE dataset, a PHQ-9-based depression detection benchmark. Using Flan T5-XL (11B) and T5-XL (11B) enhanced with the CREST framework, the authors reported a 6% improvement in PHQ-9 answerability and a 21% improvement in BLEURT scores compared to GPT 3.5. The study highlights that while standard LLMs exhibit high sensitivity to prompt rephrasing (simulated via 1,000 iterations per sample), the Neurosymbolic approach stabilizes sentiment analysis and logical coherence. The authors distinguish between system-level explainability (post-hoc attention maps) and user-level explainability (inherent knowledge grounding), arguing the latter is essential for clinician trust. However, the authors note limitations in current reliability metrics, suggesting future work must develop quantitative measures beyond Cohen’s Kappa for knowledge-grounded systems. The paper concludes that while LLMs are versatile, trustworthiness requires the structural grounding provided by Neurosymbolic integration, particularly for high-stakes domains, and outlines future plans to test CREST on knowledge-intensive benchmarks like HELM.
