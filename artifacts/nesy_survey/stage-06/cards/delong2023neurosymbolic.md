# Neurosymbolic AI for Reasoning Over Knowledge Graphs: A Survey

*source: s2_fulltext | tokens: 18524*

DeLong et al. (2023) present a specialized survey on neurosymbolic AI for reasoning over Knowledge Graphs (KGs), distinguishing it from general neurosymbolic reviews by focusing on the integration of symbolic logic with neural embeddings for tasks like KG completion and link prediction. The authors propose a novel taxonomy categorizing methods into three distinct architectures based on the interaction between symbolic and neural modules. The first category, *logically-informed embeddings* (e.g., Walking RDF and OWL, KGE*, UniKER), uses symbolic inference to augment the KG prior to neural processing. The second, *embeddings with logical constraints* (e.g., R2N, SLRE, KALE, ReOnto), imposes logic as regularization or loss functions during training. The third, *rule learning approaches* (e.g., Neural LP, DRUM, RNNLogic, PoLo), dynamically learns logical rules and confidences within an end-to-end framework.

The survey evaluates these approaches against five key characteristics: interpretability, guided training, handling underrepresented types, heterogeneous aggregation, and long-range dependencies. It notes that while traditional rule-based methods (e.g., AMIE, SAFRAN) offer inherent interpretability, they struggle with scalability and performance compared to black-box embedding methods (e.g., TransE, DistMult). Neurosymbolic hybrids aim to mitigate this trade-off. Benchmarks span general KGs like YAGO and DBpedia to domain-specific graphs such as Hetionet and biomedical COVID-19 KGs. Path-based methods like PoLo and LNN-MP are highlighted for their ability to capture long-range dependencies in biological networks, while iterative methods like KALE address data sparsity.

The authors critically assess limitations, noting that performance improvements in stacked models may sometimes result from ensemble effects rather than true neurosymbolic synergy. Other challenges include the high computational cost of rule grounding, the scarcity of expert-curated ontologies for new domains, and the lack of standardized interpretability metrics. Future directions proposed include multimodal data integration, reasoning over conditional edge types, spatiotemporal reasoning, and few-shot learning for rare relation types. The survey concludes that while neurosymbolic KG reasoning is maturing, particularly for safety-critical domains like biomedicine, further work is needed to standardize evaluation and ensure scalability for large-scale graphs.
