# KLay: Accelerating Arithmetic Circuits for Neurosymbolic AI

*source: arxiv_html | tokens: 9328*

Maene et al. address the critical computational bottleneck in neurosymbolic AI where arithmetic circuits, used to enforce symbolic constraints on neural networks, are inefficient on modern GPUs due to irregular sparsity. While arithmetic circuits enable end-to-end differentiable probabilistic inference (e.g., via d-DNNF compilation), standard node-by-node traversal fails to utilize parallel tensor hardware, often forcing CPU offloading. The authors propose KLAY (Knowledge Layers), a data structure that maps arithmetic circuits into layers of nodes evaluated via index and scatter-reduce operations native to tensor libraries like PyTorch and Jax.

The approach involves three key algorithms: layerization (grouping nodes by height to enable parallel evaluation), node deduplication (using Merkle hashes to merge equivalent sub-circuits), and tensorized evaluation (converting graph traversal into vectorized operations). This allows the "compile once, evaluate often" paradigm to leverage GPU parallelism without custom CUDA kernels. Crucially, the method supports the logarithmic semiring for numerical stability and handles multi-rooted circuits for batched training.

Empirical evaluation on an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090 demonstrates speedups of multiple orders of magnitude over state-of-the-art baselines. On synthetic 3-CNF benchmarks compiled to SDDs, KLAY outperforms PySDD by over one order of magnitude on large circuits. In neurosymbolic tasks, including Sudoku, Grid pathfinding, and Warcraft, KLAY improves gradient computation runtime by up to four orders of magnitude compared to naive PyTorch implementations. Specifically, on the MNIST-addition task within DeepProbLog, KLAY achieves two orders of magnitude speedup over the standard implementation and Scallop, while maintaining exact inference. Notably, KLAY also outperforms Juice, a related layerization framework, which the authors found to be slower on GPUs than its CPU counterpart due to missed parallelization opportunities.

The work refutes prior claims by Shah et al. and Vasimuddin et al. that arithmetic circuits are too sparse for efficient GPU execution. Unlike Juice, which relies on custom SIMD/CUDA kernels, KLAY is hardware-agnostic, relying on compiler stacks for optimization. The primary limitation remains the #P-hard knowledge compilation step, though this is amortized over repeated evaluations. KLAY effectively bridges the gap between symbolic tractability and neural hardware efficiency, facilitating the scaling of neurosymbolic models to larger real-world applications.
