# Neurosymbolic AI: the 3rd wave

*source: arxiv_pdf | tokens: 18839*

d'Avila Garcez and Lamb (2020) argue that Neurosymbolic AI (NSAI) constitutes the '3rd wave' of artificial intelligence, necessary to resolve the trust, safety, and interpretability deficits of current deep learning (DL) systems. Positioning themselves within the ongoing debate between connectionist and symbolic paradigms, the authors invoke Daniel Kahneman’s 'System 1' (intuitive, DL-based) and 'System 2' (deliberative, symbolic) to frame the need for hybrid architectures. They contend that while DL excels at perception and pattern recognition, it lacks robust reasoning, extrapolation capabilities, and compositional generalization, often failing on out-of-distribution data.

The paper synthesizes two decades of neural-symbolic computing research, proposing a taxonomy of integration based on Henry Kautz’s AAAI 2020 lecture. This framework categorizes systems from Type 1 (symbolic inputs/outputs) to Type 6 (fully integrated symbolic reasoning within a neural engine). Key examples cited include Logic Tensor Networks (LTN), which map first-order logic constraints into differentiable loss functions to guide learning, and hybrid approaches like DeepProbLog and the Neuro-Symbolic Concept Learner, which couple neural perception with symbolic inference trees. The authors emphasize the distinction between loose coupling (hybrid) and tight integration (compiling logic into weights or constraints), noting that current neural networks are largely 'propositional' and struggle with full first-order logic without symbolic assistance. They argue for a bridge between distributed representations (neural) and localist representations (symbolic) to enable both efficient learning and precise reasoning.

Identifying critical gaps, the authors highlight the need for principled knowledge extraction to achieve explainability and counterfactual reasoning aligned with Judea Pearl’s causal hierarchy. They point to commonsense reasoning tasks, such as those in the COPA dataset, as a benchmark where DL fails without symbolic grounding. The paper concludes by outlining three major challenges for the next decade: (1) sound and efficient extraction of first-order logic from large networks, (2) goal-directed combinatorial reasoning over learned representations, and (3) establishing human-network communication protocols for accountability. Specifically, they note the duality where learning universal quantification is hard but reasoning existential quantification is hard, suggesting complementary strengths. They also stress modularity as a key ingredient to avoid the loss of interpretability in monolithic networks. Ultimately, the authors call for standardized benchmarks and a shift from 'neurons versus symbols' to a unified framework where distributed learning and symbolic reasoning coexist to support robust, trustworthy AI.
