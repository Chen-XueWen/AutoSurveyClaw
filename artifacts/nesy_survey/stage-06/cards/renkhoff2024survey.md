# A Survey on Verification and Validation, Testing and Evaluations of Neurosymbolic Artificial Intelligence

*source: s2_fulltext | tokens: 19343*

Renkhoff et al. (2024) provide a comprehensive survey on Verification and Validation (V&V) and Testing & Evaluation (T&E) within Neurosymbolic Artificial Intelligence (NSAI), published in IEEE Transactions on Artificial Intelligence. Addressing the "black box" limitations of deep learning (DL), the authors argue that NSAI's integration of symbolic logic can facilitate safer, more trustworthy systems. The survey critically evaluates two primary taxonomies for NSAI architectures: Kautz’s six-category design (e.g., Symbolic Neurosymbolic, Neuro[symbolic]) and Yu et al.’s three-category interaction model (Learning for Reasoning, Reasoning for Learning, Learning-Reasoning).

The authors map standard V&V concepts—validity and soundness—to symbolic components (propositional/first-order logic, Knowledge Graphs) and sub-symbolic components (DL). For symbolic verification, they note that propositional logic is decidable via truth tables, whereas first-order logic is undecidable but manageable with tools like semantic tableaux. Knowledge Graph validation is assessed through tools like COPAAL and DeepFact. For sub-symbolic validation, the survey references Huang et al. (2020), covering robustness, reachability, and interpretability via methods such as DeepXplore, DLFuzz, and influence functions.

Concrete case studies illustrate the interplay between V&V and architecture. The REVEL framework for neurosymbolic reinforcement learning utilizes a symbolic safety shield, outperforming Deep Deterministic Policy Gradients (DDPG) in 7 out of 10 benchmarks and Constrained Policy Optimization (CPO) in 4 out of 10. DeepProbLog is highlighted for tasks like digit addition and sorting on the MNIST dataset, demonstrating improved sample efficiency over pure DL. AlphaGo Zero is analyzed as a "Learning for Reasoning" system where the symbolic Monte Carlo Tree Search relies on the neural network’s state evaluation. Additionally, zero-shot recognition via semantic embeddings (using NELL, NEIL, and ImageNet datasets) demonstrates robustness against KG noise but highlights the need for GCN-specific verification.

The survey concludes that while current V&V methods apply independently to NSAI components, dedicated frameworks for the hybrid system are largely absent. The authors identify open challenges, including the scalability of logic verification and the need for testing emerging architectures like Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) within NSAI contexts. They advocate for leveraging symbolic rules to constrain sub-symbolic predictions to enhance safety and interpretability in critical domains, noting that future research must focus on domain-specific testing frameworks and efficiency comparisons against conventional DL approaches.
