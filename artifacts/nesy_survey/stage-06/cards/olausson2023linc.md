# LINC: A Neurosymbolic Approach for Logical Reasoning by Combining Language Models with First-Order Logic Provers

*source: s2_fulltext | tokens: 21153*

Olausson et al. (EMNLP 2023) present LINC (Logical Inference via Neurosymbolic Computation), a neurosymbolic framework designed to enhance logical reasoning in Large Language Models (LLMs). Addressing the unreliability of end-to-end LLM reasoning, particularly regarding negation and long chains, LINC reformulates the task as a modular pipeline: an LLM acts as a semantic parser translating natural language premises into First-Order Logic (FOL) expressions (formatted via the Python NLTK logic module), which are then evaluated by an external symbolic theorem prover (Prover9). To mitigate parsing errors, the system employs a K-way majority voting strategy (K=10).

The authors evaluate LINC against baselines (Naïve, Scratchpad, Chain-of-Thought) using three models: StarCoder+ (15.5B), GPT-3.5, and GPT-4, across two datasets: FOLIO (expert-written, open-domain) and ProofWriter (synthetic, Open-World Assumption). On ProofWriter, LINC demonstrates significant scalability; while Chain-of-Thought performance degrades as proof depth increases, LINC maintains near-perfect accuracy (98.3% for GPT-4) across all depths. Notably, augmenting the smaller StarCoder+ with LINC yields an 82.5% accuracy, outperforming GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 CoT by absolute margins of 38% and 10%, respectively. On FOLIO, LINC improves StarCoder+ accuracy to 56.0% (vs. 41.8% for CoT), though GPT-4 LINC scores slightly lower than GPT-4 CoT (72.5% vs. 75.3%), a difference found statistically insignificant.

Error analysis reveals distinct failure modes: LINC struggles with lossy semantic formalization (missing implicit info or syntax errors), whereas CoT fails due to logical fallacies (e.g., converse errors) or getting stuck in reasoning chains. Crucially, the methods exhibit complementary error patterns, with low overlap in mispredictions (similarity score ~0.14 vs. 0.56 for CoT baselines). The work positions itself alongside concurrent neurosymbolic efforts like Logic-LM and SATLM, distinguishing itself through a focus on error analysis and scalability across proof depths. Limitations include computational costs due to API calls and voting, and the NP-hard nature of general FOL deduction which may impact scalability. Future work should address implicit premise discovery and syntax-constrained generation to improve performance on complex natural language inputs.
