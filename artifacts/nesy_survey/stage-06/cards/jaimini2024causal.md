# Causal Neurosymbolic AI: A Synergy Between Causality and Neurosymbolic Methods

*source: abstract_only | tokens: 106*

This paper introduces **Causal Neurosymbolic AI (Causal NeSy)** as a distinct subfield, arguing that the current Neurosymbolic AI (NeSyAI) paradigm requires a deeper integration of causality to move beyond predictive accuracy. The authors classify this work as a **Position/Theory paper**, as it establishes a conceptual framework rather than presenting a specific trained model or empirical benchmark results. The central argument posits that while existing NeSy systems successfully combine neural learning with symbolic reasoning, they often remain confined to correlational patterns, limiting their utility in high-stakes decision-making.

The technical approach is defined by three specific synergies. First, the framework enriches NeSy systems with **explicit representations of causality**, allowing the model to distinguish between correlation and causation. Second, it proposes an integration mechanism where **causal knowledge is combined with domain knowledge**, ensuring that the symbolic constraints reflect not just logical rules but also causal dependencies. Third, it enables the application of NeSy techniques specifically for **causal AI tasks**, such as counterfactual reasoning or intervention analysis.

In the context of a Neurosymbolic AI survey, this work addresses a critical gap: the lack of causal reasoning capabilities in current NeSy benchmarks. Prior NeSy approaches, such as those utilizing differentiable logic or tensor-based symbolic reasoning, typically optimize for loss minimization on observational data. Causal NeSy diverges by prioritizing structural causal models (SCMs) within the symbolic layer. The paper does not cite specific model architectures (e.g., DeepProbLog or NS-Net) or numerical results, as its contribution is definitional. Instead, it claims that explicit causal representation yields insights that predictive models fail to analyze from observational data.

Key limitations include the dependency on obtaining accurate causal knowledge, which is often difficult to acquire in real-world scenarios. The authors imply that future work must address the automation of causal discovery within the neurosymbolic loop. For the literature survey, this paper serves as a pivot point, suggesting that the next generation of NeSy benchmarks should prioritize intervention-based metrics over simple classification accuracy. It challenges the field to move from "what" happens to "why" it happens, setting a theoretical baseline for future empirical work in causal neurosymbolic systems. Ultimately, this work defines the boundary conditions for NeSy systems to be considered robust enough for causal inference tasks.
