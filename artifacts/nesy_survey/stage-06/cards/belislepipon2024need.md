# Why we need to be careful with LLMs in medicine

This position paper argues for a cautious adoption of Large Language Models (LLMs) in healthcare, emphasizing the critical gap between generative potential and clinical reliability. While acknowledging the utility of LLMs for medical documentation and workflow efficiency, the authors highlight significant risks associated with unverified deployment in high-stakes environments. The paper identifies key use cases where LLMs are currently proposed, including diagnostic assistance and patient communication, noting that models like ChatGPT-3.5 exhibit strong alignment with human language patterns (Harrer, 2023; Ayers et al.). However, the central argument posits that this linguistic fluency does not equate to medical accuracy or safety.

The authors contend that the "black-box" nature of current LLMs poses significant liability. Unlike neurosymbolic systems which integrate logical constraints and symbolic reasoning, standard LLMs lack inherent mechanisms for fact-checking or adherence to established medical guidelines, leading to potential hallucinations. The paper suggests that without rigorous validation frameworks, reliance on these models could compromise patient safety. This critique directly informs the Neurosymbolic AI survey by underscoring a primary motivation for hybrid approaches: the need for verifiable reasoning in critical domains. The authors imply that future work must prioritize interpretability and error bounds over raw generative capability.

It critiques the prioritization of efficiency gains (e.g., managing documentation) over diagnostic integrity.
