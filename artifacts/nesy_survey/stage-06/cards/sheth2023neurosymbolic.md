# Neurosymbolic AI - Why, What, and How

*source: arxiv_pdf | tokens: 5744*

Sheth, Roy, and Gaur’s "Neurosymbolic AI - Why, What, and How" serves as a foundational position paper introducing the neurosymbolic AI paradigm, arguing for the integration of neural perception with symbolic cognition to overcome the limitations of standalone approaches. The authors frame the necessity of this integration through Kahneman’s System 1 (perception) and System 2 (cognition) analogy: while neural networks excel at large-scale pattern recognition (System 1), they lack the explicit knowledge structures required for reasoning, analogy, and planning (System 2). Consequently, pure neural systems struggle with explainability and safety, particularly in critical domains like healthcare and autonomous driving.

The paper proposes a comprehensive taxonomy of neurosymbolic techniques categorized by how they integrate neural and symbolic components. The first category involves "lowering," where structured symbolic knowledge is compressed to integrate with neural patterns. This includes knowledge graph embedding methods (e.g., K-Adapter) and logic-based representations (e.g., Logical Neural Networks). The second category involves "lifting," where information is extracted from neural patterns to map to symbolic knowledge. This includes federated pipeline methods (e.g., Langchain, Wolfram Alpha integration) and intertwined end-to-end differentiable methods (e.g., Process Knowledge-infused Learning or PK-iL).

Empirical support is drawn from a mental health diagnostic assistance use case utilizing PK-iL. In this implementation, the neurosymbolic pipeline achieved 70% expert satisfaction regarding response quality and adherence to clinical guidelines, significantly outperforming standard LLMs like text-Davinci-003, which scored 47%. The authors note that while federated pipelines offer high scalability and user explainability, they are limited by the language model's ability to parse domain constraints. Conversely, end-to-end differentiable methods (Category 2b) score highest across algorithmic and application-level metrics, including abstraction, planning, and safety constraints.
